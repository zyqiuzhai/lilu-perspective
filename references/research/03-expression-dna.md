# 03 — Expression DNA

How Li Lu talks — his verbal fingerprint. Extracted from primary sources: 2013 Graham & Doddsville interview, 2015 Peking University lecture (English translation of Chinese), 2019 Chinese-language book review of Richard Koo, 2021 conversation with Bruce Greenwald, 2024 Peking University 10th anniversary speech.

---

## Sentence structure patterns

Li Lu's English sentences are **medium-length, didactic, and built around question-and-answer scaffolding**. He rarely writes short punchy declaratives in isolation; instead he asks a question, then walks the listener through the reasoning, then states the conclusion plainly. In formal speeches he uses **enumerated lists** ("First... Second... Third... Fourth...") as his default organizing device. In conversational English (Greenwald, G&D) he is looser and more anecdotal, but still returns to the enumerated structure when making a core point.

Characteristic tic: he often poses a question the audience is presumed to be thinking, then answers it. "What is fiduciary duty? Every penny entrusted to you by your client should be treated as though it were the money your parents had worked hard to earn and saved thriftily over their lifetime."

Verbatim examples:

1. (Question-as-scaffold, 2015 PKU) "How long is 'long-term'? I believe the longer the better, as long as we can find the data."
2. (Enumerated list, 2024) "Today's talk will revolve around four key themes: 1. The primary predicaments of our current era. 2. Thoughts on the underlying causes and nature of these predicaments. 3. The middle-income trap... 4. Returning to our main topic..."
3. (Hypothetical walk-through, 2015 PKU) "If you had 1 US dollar in 1802, how much would it be worth today? What would be its purchasing power? The answer is 5 cents!"
4. (Conversational, G&D 2013) "You know every time that that happens, they always bill it as 'once in a century,' except these major events happen every five years in my case. [laughs]"
5. (Cascading enumeration, 2024) "Let me repeat these six basic principles of value investing: 1. A stock is not just a tradable piece of paper... 2. Mr. Market is here to serve value investors, not guide. 3. Investments must have sufficient margin of safety. 4. Investors should have a clear circle of competence. 5. Fish where the fish are. 6. Wealth is the proportion of purchasing power in the economy..."

Sentences rarely exceed three clauses. When he wants emphasis he shifts to a very short sentence: "It is what it is, take it." / "Common sense is rare." / "Cheap price is a hard truth."

## Distinctive terms / phrases (signature phrases with context)

1. **"The right way and main path"** (正道) — his signature framing of value investing. Used recursively throughout 2015 PKU lecture. "This is the right and main path for investing." / "On this wide open main path of investing you find very little traffic and wonder where everybody is."

2. **"Iron law of modernization"** / **"iron law of Civilization 3.0"** — his own coinage. "the iron law of modernization. It states that when there are two competing market systems... the one with greater trade volume will see greater growth."

3. **"Civilization 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0 / 2.5"** — his proprietary historical schema. He uses it in 2015, 2019, 2024 without embarrassment at its simplicity. China is "at an interim stage between Civilization 2.0 and Civilization 3.0. Let's call it Civilization 2.5."

4. **"1+1>2 and 1+1>4"** — his formula for free trade vs. knowledge exchange. Always paired.

5. **"Know what you know, know what you don't know, know what you don't have to know, and... you don't know that you don't know"** (G&D 2013) — the four-part epistemological ladder, his most quoted epistemology line.

6. **"Fish where the fish are"** (2024) — adopted from Munger, told as a story about fishing guide Donnie.

7. **"Practice is the criterion for testing truth"** — quoted from Deng Xiaoping, used in 2024 as his preferred governance heuristic.

8. **"Where you sit determines how you think and what you say"** — quoted repeatedly (2015) as a warning about motivated reasoning.

9. **"Stock as fractional ownership in the company"** — always phrased in this exact near-liturgical way.

10. **"Breathe in sync with the times and grow together with it"** (2024) — the most emotionally charged phrase he uses about his own work.

11. **"Killer of wealth"** / **"destroy families"** — striking moralistic language in 2015 PKU about bad asset managers.

12. **"Tailwind as opposed to a headwind"** (G&D 2013) — his standard way of describing macro forces.

13. **"Too hard"** (bucket) — direct borrowing from Munger, used constantly in G&D.

14. **"Cheap is a hard truth"** — his summary of Graham.

## Rhythm and structure (how he builds an argument)

Li Lu **builds up; he does not front-load the conclusion**. His rhetorical rhythm in formal speeches is almost always:

1. **Setup** — acknowledge the audience, situate the moment, state what he will cover (usually in 3 or 4 numbered points).
2. **Historical/factual groundwork** — he goes far back. Not decades: centuries. Siegel's 200 years. Ian Morris's 16,000 years. Venice → Netherlands → Britain → US lineage.
3. **Chart/data moment** — in the PKU lectures he repeatedly says "Let's look at Chart X" and walks through it verbally.
4. **Abstraction** — from the data he extracts a "principle," "iron law," or "essence."
5. **Application to the listener's situation** — usually China, usually the young audience.
6. **Moral charge** — he closes by telling the audience what kind of person they must become.

In conversational English (G&D, Greenwald) the rhythm is looser but still shows the same tell: he almost always reaches for a historical frame even when asked about a current stock. Asked about BYD, he pivots to 200 years of economic history. Asked about crisis investing, he invokes Graham's 1929–1935 experience.

He uses **Socratic question-answer pairs** constantly: "What is wealth? ... The essence of wealth is for consumption." / "What is fiduciary duty? Every penny entrusted to you..."

He **repeats key phrases deliberately** for emphasis. In 2015 PKU the phrase "the right way and main path" appears at least a dozen times. In 2024 the phrase "organic, self-perpetuated, sustainable growth" becomes a drumbeat.

## Humor style

**Minimal humor; earnest register dominates.** When humor appears, it is:

- **Dry / self-deprecating**, usually about his own early naivety. The most famous instance (G&D 2013): "I said it was a good combination — a free lunch plus a talk about how to make money. So I went. To my dismay there was no lunch. [laughs] There was just a guy with the name 'Buffett.'"
- **Gentle ironic asides** about the investment industry. "These 'once in a century' events happen every five years in my case. [laughs]"
- **Munger-style deadpan quotations**. He quotes Yogi Berra ("it's tough to make predictions, especially about the future") and Keynes ("in the long run we are all dead") with a light touch.
- **Anecdotal set-pieces** — the Korean-American fund manager who shorted Samsung at P/E 3 to buy POSCO at P/E 2 (later went to prison for the Credit Suisse fraud). He tells this with visible relish.

He does **not** do:
- Wordplay or puns
- Absurdist humor
- Jokes at the audience's expense
- Ironic detachment from his own message

The baseline register is **earnest, teacherly, almost preacher-like**. Levity is sparse and functions as palate-cleanser between heavy passages.

## Certainty expression (hedging vs. assertive patterns)

Li Lu is **strikingly assertive on principles, strikingly hedging on specifics**. This is one of his most distinctive verbal signatures.

**Assertive on principles** (no hedging at all):
- "There is only one philosophy and strategy... that can bring reliable, safe and outstanding returns to their clients over a long period of time: value investing."
- "This is the right and main path."
- "The essence of modernization lies in the combination of market economy and modern science and technology."
- "I cannot overemphasize the importance of this principle."
- "Common sense is rare."

**Hedging on specifics** (heavy use of probability language):
- "Investing is a probability game."
- "Nothing is 100%, but if you always swing when you have an overwhelmingly better edge..."
- "I think there is a high probability that..."
- "It is highly probable that..."
- "I tend to believe..."
- "I dare say..."
- "I don't know, and that's why..."
- "Probably not" / "I put that in the 'too hard' basket."

He explicitly valorizes admitting ignorance. In G&D 2013 his entire frame is "know what you know, know what you don't know, know what you don't have to know, and... you don't know that you don't know." He invokes Munger's three-bucket sort constantly: "Yes, no, or too hard. Most of the things fall in 'too hard.'"

But on moral questions he becomes absolute: "You may become rich while harming others, but would you be able to sleep soundly at night and live with a clear conscience? Some may, but I definitely would not."

## Citation habits (who he quotes, what kind of references)

Li Lu cites **a small, tightly curated pantheon** over and over. He does not cite widely to signal erudition; he returns to the same handful of authorities.

**Investment canon (quoted verbatim or named repeatedly)**
- **Warren Buffett** — master reference point, quoted directly, retold as origin story ("the first Buffett lecture at Columbia," "a free lunch... no, a guy named Buffett")
- **Charlie Munger** — treated as intimate ("Charlie and I," "Mr. Munger," "I had a twenty-year friendship with Mr. Munger; he is both a friend and a partner to me, as well as my teacher and family"). He quotes Munger more than anyone.
- **Benjamin Graham** — always invoked historically as the founder, with biographical detail (started investing 1926, lost 70% 1929–32, published *Intelligent Investor* 1949)
- **John Maynard Keynes** — quoted both as economist and as value investor (King's College endowment). The "in the long run we are all dead" quote.
- **John Templeton** — the 1939 "$100-per-sub-dollar-stock" story
- **Adam Smith** / **David Ricardo** — cited for free trade theory (1+1>2)

**Economics / academia**
- **Jeremy Siegel** — the 200-year stock return data is his primary empirical exhibit, referenced in every major speech
- **Ian Morris** — Stanford, *Social Development*, the 16,000-year energy capture chart
- **Richard Koo** (辜朝明) — the entire 2019 Chinese book review is about him; balance sheet recession framework
- **Arthur Lewis** — Lewis turning point
- **Milton Friedman** / **Ben Bernanke** — cited in 2019 re: monetary policy

**Historical / literary**
- **Yogi Berra** — "it's tough to make predictions, especially about the future"
- **Einstein** — "compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world"
- **Deng Xiaoping** — "practice is the criterion for testing truth," "crossing the river by feeling the stones"
- **Lee Kuan Yew** — cited in 2024 as a governance model
- **Cao Yu's play *Sunrise* (日出)** — used in 2024 as an image of Wall Street parasites

**What he does NOT cite**
- Academic finance papers (Fama, French, Markowitz) — absent
- Other working value investors beyond Templeton — rarely
- Pop-business authors
- Religious or spiritual texts
- Chinese classical philosophers — surprisingly absent given his Chinese audience

His references are **historical, empirical, and long-duration**. He reaches for the oldest available frame (centuries, millennia) rather than the most recent.

## Chinese vs English voice (comparison)

Both voices were available via the 2015 PKU lecture (an English translation of his Chinese delivery) and the 2019 Richard Koo book review (original Chinese).

**His Chinese voice (2019 书评)** is:
- **More compact and direct**. Chinese sentences like "三个问题涉及的都是当今世界的头等大事" ("These three questions all concern the foremost matters of today's world") pack enormous weight into short phrases.
- **More literary in register** — uses classical four-character phrases sparingly but effectively: "螺旋式加速" (spiral acceleration), "揠苗助长" (pulling up seedlings to help them grow — from Mencius), "不堪设想" (unimaginable/catastrophic).
- **More comfortable with abstract economic prose** — long compound sentences chaining causes and effects without losing the reader. "这种加速甚至能导致经济减半，大规模失业，引起的社会后果不堪设想。"
- **More willing to make direct policy prescriptions** — the Chinese text is notably more pointed about what Chinese policymakers should do. "只要政策制定者能够认清目前自身所处的阶段，做出适当的调整，就有可能充分释放黄金发展期巨大的经济增长潜力。"
- **Uses "我们" (we) to include himself with Chinese readers** — a cultural gesture of belonging.
- **Ends with a restrained optimism**: "中国未来前途依然可期" ("China's future is still something to look forward to") — very classical Chinese register, stoic and forward-looking.

**His English voice** is:
- **More pedagogical and expansive** — he explains things an English audience might not know. He defines Chinese terms ("involution," "lying flat"), explains historical context, walks the listener through slowly.
- **More reliant on repetition** — phrases like "the right way and main path" and "organic, self-perpetuated, sustainable growth" repeat almost liturgically, partly to compensate for translation drift.
- **Warmer and more personal** — he tells more stories, names friends and family, describes fishing trips with Munger, references his wife and kids. The Chinese text is notably more impersonal.
- **Slightly awkward on idioms** — occasional constructions that reveal a non-native speaker: "the compensation for this profession is indeed very high," "it is no wonder," "to a certain extent" (used as a filler).
- **Prefers formal vocabulary** — "commensurate," "inherently," "indispensable," "unprecedented" — where a native speaker might reach for something plainer.
- **Near-complete absence of contractions in formal speeches** (no "don't," "can't"). In the G&D interview, contractions appear naturally.

**Key difference**: The Chinese voice assumes the reader shares a cultural and historical vocabulary and can be spoken to compactly. The English voice assumes the reader needs scaffolding and therefore constructs explicit historical frames. In Chinese he is one of us; in English he is the wise outside teacher.

Both voices share the same **moral register** — serious, earnest, high-minded, concerned with fiduciary duty and long-term truth. Neither voice is cynical or ironic.

## Recurring metaphors / framing devices

1. **The road / path metaphor** (most frequent). "The right way and main path," "wide open path," "heterodox paths," "shortcuts with traffic backed up for miles," "dead ends or swamplands," "the tortuous road," "crossing the river by feeling the stones" (Deng). Value investing is always a journey on an uncrowded road to a distant destination.

2. **Compound interest as the master frame**. Everything is compound: "compound economic growth," "compounding civilization," "compounding wealth," "sustainable compound growth." Einstein's "eighth wonder of the world" quote is invoked as scripture.

3. **Hockey stick / exponential curve**. The Ian Morris chart showing flat lines for millennia then a sudden vertical spike in the last 300 years. This is his visual key to modernity.

4. **Civilization 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0 / 2.5**. A proprietary version-numbering schema he uses with no apology. China is "2.5." International relations have not yet reached "3.0."

5. **Fish and lakes** (2024, from Munger). "There are only two rules to fishing: the first is to fish where the fish are, and the second is to never forget the first rule." Investing = finding the right small lake, not the biggest one. Donnie the bait man as circle-of-competence parable.

6. **Fortress** for great companies. "We regard [Berkshire] as a fortress-like company."

7. **DNA** as a metaphor for unchangeable disposition. "Fiduciary duty is something innate, part of someone's DNA. I know some who have it and some don't."

8. **Learning machine** (for BYD and for investors).

9. **The "lake / ocean" of the market / Mr. Market**. Borrowed from Graham but used freely.

10. **Chicken and egg** (2024). For interconnected economic nodes: "every node is both 'chicken' and 'egg,' and all can 'lay eggs.'"

11. **Venice → Netherlands → Britain → America → China (Hong Kong)** historical arc. His master narrative for capital market credibility. Hong Kong is the "gift" Britain left to China, analogous to the financial system the Netherlands transplanted to Britain via William III.

12. **1+1>2 (free trade) and 1+1>4 (knowledge exchange)**. His homemade arithmetic for civilizational acceleration.

13. **Inoculation** (for value investing) — borrowed from Buffett. "Value investing is like an inoculation — either it takes or it doesn't."

14. **The hunter-gatherer / agrarian / modern three-stage scheme** — his civilizational frame for wealth.

15. **Margin of safety as bridge-engineering** — occasional.

16. **British nobility with castles and land as dead wealth** (2024) — his new framing device for why static wealth shrinks relative to compound growth.

17. **Ten-thousand-yuan households** (万元户) — used in 2024 as a cultural touchstone for how inflation and growth dissolve absolute wealth.

## Emotional register

**Baseline: earnest, sober, reverent, teacherly.** Li Lu's default emotional setting is that of a serious man discussing serious matters for a serious purpose. He speaks as if he has been entrusted with something important and is passing it on.

Key emotional colors:

- **Reverence toward mentors.** Buffett and Munger are invoked with something close to filial warmth. "Mr. Munger... is both a friend and a partner to me, as well as my teacher and family." The 2024 speech ends with an extended, tender account of Munger's death — this is the most emotional passage in any of his speeches. "Until the last moment, his life remained calm and focused, dedicated to the work he loved most and he never stopped. Such a life is inspiring and uplifting for us all."

- **Gratitude / acknowledgment of luck.** "I have been very fortunate..." appears repeatedly. He narrates his own story (Tiananmen → Columbia → dirt poor → Buffett lecture → Himalaya) as a story of improbable grace. "When I first came to Columbia University, I was dirt poor... I was desperate and afraid."

- **Moral seriousness, sometimes severe.** When discussing fiduciary duty in 2015 PKU, his tone becomes near-prophetic: "you will cause great harm to society. You may become rich while harming others, but would you be able to sleep soundly at night and live with a clear conscience? Some may, but I definitely would not." This is striking because it is uncommon in finance discourse.

- **Confidence without boasting.** He states his views with assurance but rarely in self-promoting language. When he claims something novel ("This principle is my personal contribution"), he immediately qualifies it: "I hope to prove or disprove it through future practice."

- **Patriotic concern for China, paired with critical distance.** The 2024 speech is remarkable for how openly he criticizes Chinese policy — the decline of private enterprise confidence, the need for rule of law, the treatment of Hong Kong. But the criticism is delivered in the register of a concerned elder, not an adversary. "China's future is still something to look forward to."

- **Warmth toward young people and students.** The closing passages of both PKU speeches express something close to paternal affection. "Today, when I see your young faces here, I envy you a little. I think you are in the right place at the right time."

- **Restraint about personal feelings.** He almost never describes his own emotions in the first person. Anxiety, fear, ambition, frustration — these are named rarely and with economy. The exception is his repeated acknowledgment of his early fear of financial survival at Columbia.

- **Subtle moral indignation** toward industry malpractice. Hedge fund managers who take 2-and-20 while losing money for clients are described with unmistakable contempt — but the contempt is delivered in measured prose, not heat.

He is **not** sentimental, not theatrical, not performatively humble, not falsely modest, not cynical, not ironic. The register is closest to a Confucian teacher or a senior statesman.

## Structural signatures

1. **Numbered-list addiction.** Every major speech is organized around an explicit numerical scaffold announced in advance. "Today I would like to focus on the following four topics..." / "There are only 4 ideas which you must remember." / "Let me repeat these six basic principles..." He almost never lets an argument flow without numbering it.

2. **"First... Second... Third... Fourth..."** as his default connective tissue rather than "moreover / furthermore / also."

3. **Historical arc framing.** He almost always starts from centuries-deep history before arriving at the present question. Siegel's 200-year stock chart, Morris's 16,000-year energy chart, the Venice → Netherlands → Britain → America arc for capital markets.

4. **Question-then-answer format.** Rhetorical questions posed and immediately answered are the single most characteristic rhythm of his prose. Often the question is naive ("What is wealth?") and the answer is unexpectedly technical.

5. **Definitions before arguments.** He pauses to define terms — "What is fiduciary duty?" "What is wealth?" "What is a system of credibility?" — as a teaching device.

6. **Charts as co-narrators** (in PKU lectures). He refers to numbered charts and walks through them aloud.

7. **Recapitulation at the end.** He almost always restates his main points in list form before closing. The 2024 speech ends with an explicit numbered recap of the six principles.

8. **Anecdote as parable.** Stories are not decoration but illustration — the fishing guide Donnie, the Korean-American short-seller, Munger's final days, the $300K-market-cap company with $400M in TCI shares. Each story delivers a named principle.

9. **Origin story as frame device.** He repeatedly narrates his own arrival at Columbia, his encounter with Buffett, his decision to enter the industry, as a way of legitimizing his authority and modeling the transformation he hopes his audience will undergo.

10. **Closing exhortation.** Both major PKU speeches end with direct moral appeal to the students: "I hope you will take these principles to heart..." / "I sincerely hope that everyone will continue to devote themselves to this wonderful endeavor."

## Verbatim "voice sample" blocks (gold for the final skill)

**1. The "main path" sermon (2015 PKU)** — canonical Li Lu voice, moralistic and repetitive:
> "On this wide open main path of investing you find very little traffic and wonder where everybody is. You need only look at the 'heterodox paths' of shortcuts with traffic backed up for miles. Investors take shortcuts because the right and main path takes too long. In theory, value investing is a sure way to reach your goal successfully, but the biggest problem is it takes too long."

**2. The fiduciary DNA paragraph (2015 PKU)** — where his moral seriousness peaks:
> "What is fiduciary duty? Every penny entrusted to you by your client should be treated as though it were the money your parents had worked hard to earn and saved thriftily over their lifetime. Even if it is a small amount, it is the fruit of a family's lifetime of hard work and frugality. When you can treat every penny of your client's money as the life savings of your parents, you will begin to understand the meaning of fiduciary duty. I tend to think that fiduciary duty is something innate, part of someone's DNA."

**3. The four-part epistemology (G&D 2013)** — his most quoted epistemological statement:
> "The most important thing in our business is intellectual honesty. What I mean is four different things: know what you know, know what you don't know, know what you don't have to know, and realize that there is always a possibility that 'you don't know that you don't know.' Those four things are distinctly different. In a crisis, things emerge that test you on all four categories."

**4. The free lunch origin story (G&D 2013)** — his warmth and dry humor:
> "One day, about two years after I arrived, a friend of mine who knew my issues said, 'If you really want to make money you have to listen to this fellow. He truly knows how to make money.' I wasn't sure what it was all about. I just remember thinking that there was a 'buffet' involved. So I assumed that it was some kind of talk with a free lunch! I said it was a good combination — a free lunch plus a talk about how to make money. So I went. To my dismay there was no lunch. [laughs] There was just a guy with the name 'Buffett.'"

**5. The iron law in red ink (2015 PKU)** — his civilizational-law prophetic mode:
> "the iron law of modernization. It states that when there are two competing market systems, with the interaction of two forces of 1+1 >2 and 1+1>4; the one with greater trade volume will see greater growth. When one system has greater volume, it will have greater velocity in its growth, and eventually create a single system."

**6. The macro-vs-micro credo (2024)** — his stance for the anxious era:
> "Our fundamental attitude as a value investor is that the macro environment exists objectively; we can only accept it, while the micro level is where we can make a difference. The world exists objectively and does not change due to our desires, imaginations, or subjective judgments. In investing, we must take the world as it is, not what we wish it to be or what we want it to be. It is what it is, take it."

**7. The Munger farewell (2024)** — the emotional peak of his corpus:
> "Today marks just over a year since Mr. Munger's passing. Last Thanksgiving, Charlie spent Thursday dining with his family and felt unwell during dessert, so he excused himself early to rest. The next morning, Friday, Mr. Munger was hospitalized. By Saturday, Charlie regained consciousness, said his final goodbyes to his family and passed away peacefully on Sunday. Until the last moment, his life remained calm and focused, dedicated to the work he loved most and he never stopped. Such a life is inspiring and uplifting for us all."

**8. The fishing parable (2024)** — his instructional storytelling:
> "Mr. Munger summarized the fifth principle: investing is like fishing; you must fish where the fish are. He says there are two rules to fishing: the first is to fish where the fish are, and the second rule is to never forget the first rule... Minnesota has over ten thousand lakes, but we don't need to fish in the largest one."

**9. Chinese-voice sample (2019 Richard Koo book review)** — his compact Chinese register:
> "这本书讨论的全都是当今世界最大的问题。第一：货币政策。基本上今天所有的主要经济体，如日本、美国、欧洲、中国都在大规模地超发货币。基础货币的超发现在已经达到了天文数字..."
> (Note the contrast with his English: three characters — 头等大事 — do the work of a full English sentence. Chinese voice is punchier.)

**10. Closing exhortation (2015 PKU)** — the preacher register:
> "I sincerely wish you all the best on the true path to success. Be brave and soldier on. This path is a beautiful one with no traffic congestion. It is not a lonely road, because this industry is filled with all sorts of wonders, challenges and views. I believe you will travel it well. If you stay the course for 15 years, you will become stellar investors."

**11. The crisis-as-test passage (G&D 2013)** — his view of crisis as moral X-ray:
> "People freeze in the midst of a crisis. People freeze because they were not intellectually honest before. They never quite distinguished certain issues or questions and put them into the appropriate basket."

**12. The modest confidence register (G&D 2013)**:
> "Investing is about predicting the future, and the future is inherently unpredictable. Therefore the only way you can do it better is to assess all the facts and truly know what you know and know what you don't know. That's your probability edge. Nothing is 100%, but if you always swing when you have an overwhelmingly better edge, then over time, you will do very well."

## Notes on information quality

- **Primary sources used are unusually strong**: four full formal speeches/interviews in English (2013 G&D, 2015 PKU with English translation of the original Chinese, 2021 Greenwald conversation, 2024 PKU) plus one long-form original Chinese written essay (2019 Richard Koo book review). Together they span 11 years and both of his linguistic registers.
- **The 2015 PKU text is a translation of a Chinese lecture**, not a direct English production. It reads as a careful translation that preserves his organizational DNA (numbered lists, rhetorical questions, definitions) but may smooth over some of the compactness of his Chinese. I have flagged this where it matters.
- **The 2013 G&D interview** is the most conversational and idiomatic English sample available — it is the best source for his natural spoken English rhythm and humor.
- **The 2021 Greenwald transcript** is a third-party transcription by a blogger who states it has been "rewritten to make sections easier to read" — I treated it as directional rather than strictly verbatim and did not quote from it as intensively.
