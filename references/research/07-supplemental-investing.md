## 2006 CBS Lecture (Columbia Business School, Bruce Greenwald's class)

### Context
- Date: 2006 (exact date unspecified)
- Setting: Guest lecture at Columbia Business School, Professor Bruce Greenwald's value investing class
- Approx word count: ~6,500 words
- Format: Lecture with Q&A, two detailed case studies

### Key investing content extracted

#### Identity & temperament of value investors
- **PRIMARY**: "Only a small number of people are value investors and that is maybe because you are genetically mutated. You are very comfortable being in the minority — and this is not natural for humans."
- **PRIMARY**: "You don't get your ideas because people agree with you, but because of evidence, logic and reason."
- Estimates ~5% of assets are managed by value investors. The stock market was not created for value investors — it was created for the other 95%.
- **PRIMARY**: "If 100% of people would be value investors. There wouldn't be a stock market. Who the hell would buy IPO? Without IPOs there wouldn't be a stock market or a secondary market. If everyone needed a big margin of safety nobody would sell."
- **PRIMARY**: "As long as humans are irrational, a value investor will find something profitable in the market."

#### Three pillars of value investing (from Graham)
- **PRIMARY**: (1) You are a business owner, not a paper holder. (2) You demand a huge margin of safety. (3) Understanding Mr. Market.
- All three derive from the business-owner mentality: margin of safety is self-defense because you can't control the business; long-term holding is natural because owners don't trade.

#### Research process / investigative journalism model
- **PRIMARY**: "You will spend most of your time being an academic researcher instead of being a professional investor. You have to spend time to be a researcher and investigative journalist with an insatiable curiosity."
- **PRIMARY**: "Learning from politics, science, technology, humanity, history, literature or even poetry. Being interested in everything will aid you to find those few insights that give you tremendous opportunities."
- Read Value Line page by page — "the best business training." First checks new low lists (all-time lows in P/E, P/B, etc.).
- For international markets, uses S&P country books page by page.
- **PRIMARY**: "You can smell opportunity. The only way to develop that smell is by reading a lot."

#### The checklist
- **PRIMARY**: Explicit checklist before buying:
  1. Is it cheap?
  2. Is the business good?
  3. Is the management trustworthy and good, or is there enough external validation?
  4. What else am I missing?
  5. Why does this opportunity exist in the market?

#### Valuation approach — speed and owner mindset
- **PRIMARY**: "If you are a business owner, don't think about per-share numbers. Train yourself to think as an owner."
- Focuses on: pre-tax, pre-interest operating earnings; deployed capital; return on deployed capital.
- Insists on mental math, no calculators: "Each page shouldn't take you more than a few minutes."
- **PRIMARY**: "Analyzing the numbers should take less than five minutes. If you are not a good analyst, you will not be a good investor."

#### Management due diligence — deep fieldwork
- **PRIMARY**: "You should be like an investigative journalist. People who founded a company have big personalities, a history and left a trail of evidence."
- Advocates going to the community, church/synagogue, befriending neighbors and associates. Spend weeks if necessary.
- For Timberland: visited stores personally, discovered fashion fad firsthand. For Hyundai: went to Korea, inspected hotels and department stores, checked recent property transactions in the neighborhood.

#### Position sizing / concentration
- **PRIMARY**: Advocates extreme concentration. When a student said "$200" out of $100, Li Lu laughed approvingly: "I like talking to this class, because you are not polluted."
- Criticizes hedge fund culture of basis-point thinking: "They use Basis Points and say: 'Don't do more than 25 basis points' ... Every number sounds big. So keep your innocence, because what you are thinking is common sense."
- **PRIMARY**: "So I put shitloads of money into it." (On Timberland — estimated 30%+ of fund based on 13F filings, possibly 60%+.)

#### Two schools of value investing
- **PRIMARY**: Tweedy Browne / Ben Graham school vs. Buffett & Munger school. Li Lu identifies with the latter.
- Graham/Tweedy approach: diversified cheap stocks, ~15% annualized returns.
- Buffett/Munger approach: "The returns will come from very few insights. In 50 years of tremendous insights into companies, there will probably only be ten or so insights."
- **PRIMARY**: "In your life you probably won't have more than five or ten insights, and you will develop them over years of study."

#### Lollapalooza effect in investing
- **PRIMARY**: "Sound, complete and accurate information combined with the conscious, subconscious and psychological factors. This is what Munger calls the Lollapalooza effect."
- Thousandfold or ten-thousandfold wealth increases come from combining multiple factors — "why should it be easy."

#### Case Study: Timberland (1998)
- Found via Value Line new-low list during Asian financial crisis.
- Quick valuation: ~$300M book value, ~$275M working capital, ~$100M real estate. Operating earnings ~$110M on ~$200M deployed capital = ~50% RODC. Trading at ~book value, ~5x P/E.
- Why cheap: Asian crisis fear (but Asia was <10% of earnings), no analyst coverage (family-controlled, no need for capital markets), shareholder lawsuits (which turned out to be frivolous — one complaint filed multiple times over withdrawn earnings guidance).
- Management quality confirmed through deep community research. Family of highest integrity.
- **PRIMARY**: "Buying cheap is important and when you do, you sit on your ass and don't do something stupid. A good business will take care of itself and your money will grow with the strength of the company."
- Result: Stock went up 7x in two years, driven by earnings growth (never above 15x P/E). Sold when institutional attention arrived (analyst meetings went from 3 people to 50+).
- Research time: "Not more than a few weeks."

#### Case Study: Hyundai Department Store H&S (~2004-2005, Korea)
- Found via S&P country book, page by page.
- Market cap ~$60M, pre-tax earnings ~$36M = ~2x P/E, book value ~$240M.
- Assets: $70M cash, hotel at $30M book (worth 3-4x), 13% stake in listed department store worth ~$80M (booked at $13M), cable companies, real estate.
- Sum-of-parts: ~$320M in assets for $60M purchase price, plus $30M annual earnings.
- Corporate governance risk (Korean chaebol structure) addressed through systematic checklist.
- Result: Department store stock went from $22 to $100; H&S went from $12 to $70 (5-6x).

#### Selling discipline
- **PRIMARY**: Early philosophy: "If I wouldn't buy at that price, I would sell."
- Evolved view: reluctant to sell truly great businesses. "If it is a good business the probability is there that it will just get better. That is the law of businesses, leaders will take a proportional amount of capital."
- Tax friction as rational reason to hold: business can deploy capital at 50-100% returns vs. paying tax on gains.
- **PRIMARY**: "Only if I have an insight I can predict it, otherwise I can't even predict what will happen tomorrow. Why would I want to sell if I do have those insights?"
- Sold Timberland because "it is not an exceptional company." Holds others he won't name that are exceptional.

#### What makes a great business (competitive advantage)
- **PRIMARY**: "They need to have a competitive advantage that is getting stronger and stronger."
- Bloomberg example analyzed in depth: switching costs (hard to learn, deeply embedded in workflow), network effects (everyone uses it), low cost relative to user's income, continuous feature addition based on user feedback, winner-take-all dynamics.
- **PRIMARY**: "Virtually all companies go through a similar transformation... from a company that came from nowhere, that little by little began to gain an advantage until they hit a breaking point and usurped the established players becoming a monopoly."
- Challenges students to find businesses Buffett hasn't bought yet (not just validate existing holdings).

#### Mistakes
- **PRIMARY**: "Everytime I failed on one of those three scores, I made a mistake. Whenever I don't have accurate and complete information or think I have an insight which turns out to not be one."
- **PRIMARY**: "My biggest mistakes were the ones where I did not make the bets."
- Specific mistake: during hedge fund period, distracted by shorting and frequent trading, missed a company he had "absolute insight into" — trading below cash, knew management, went up 50-100x.
- Sometimes buys before finishing research (Timberland at $28), then buys more after confirming thesis. Accepts 20-30% losses when wrong.

#### On shorting
- **PRIMARY**: Quotes Munger: shorting is "like going to an ass-kicking contest with your hands tied behind your back."
- "If your upside is 100% and your downside is unlimited you have to trade, and as a result your mentality changes. I stopped gaining and acting on insights."
- Had someone handle shorting in Julian Robertson's office — "turned out to be pretty useless."

#### On analyst requirements
- **PRIMARY**: "If you are an analyst I only need two things. Accurate and complete information."
- Never hired anyone from business school or asset management — "they are much easier to train" (referring to people without those backgrounds).

#### Circle of competence / cumulative knowledge
- **PRIMARY**: "15 years ago I studied American companies, and now I find the Asian counterpart. Only by studying the company and industry for 15 years can I be sure to invest in them."
- Knowledge compounds: "This process had a cumulative effect, so now I am able to find and process opportunities much faster."
- Learns from wife's PhD in biology, from everything — all knowledge eventually feeds investing insight.

#### Patience and activity rate
- **PRIMARY**: "There are years where you don't have a single opportunity and years where there are many. It depends on your circle of competence and the right valuation. They don't come at a steady pace."
- **PRIMARY**: "It is ok to have a year where you don't invest into a company, it is not ok to waste my time not learning and not creating a new insight."

## 2019 Richard Koo Book Review ("The Other Half of Macroeconomics and the Fate of Globalization")

### Context
- Date: November 19, 2019
- Setting: Li Lu's annual book review (published essay)
- Language: Chinese (Mandarin)
- Approx word count: ~4,500 words (Chinese)
- Focus: Macroeconomic framework review — Koo's balance sheet recession theory, stages of economic development, and policy implications

### Key investing content extracted

This source is overwhelmingly macro-economic and policy-oriented. Investing-relevant content is limited but present:

#### Asset bubbles and their aftermath — investor awareness
- **PRIMARY**: Li Lu describes the pattern: when domestic investment opportunities dry up (stage 3 / "being-chased" economies), capital flows into bubble-prone areas — real estate, stocks, bonds, derivatives — creating massive bubbles and subsequent collapses. This happened in Japan, the US, and Western Europe sequentially.
- Implication for investors: understanding which developmental stage an economy is in determines whether asset prices reflect genuine growth or bubble dynamics.

#### Balance sheet recession as investing mental model
- **PRIMARY**: Koo's core insight as Li Lu presents it: after bubble collapse, the entire private sector shifts from profit-maximization to debt-minimization. No matter how much money central banks print, private actors save and repay debt rather than invest. This explains why massive QE failed to produce inflation or growth.
- Investing relevance: conventional valuation frameworks (earnings growth, capital deployment) break down when an entire economy is in balance-sheet-repair mode. The investor must recognize this regime shift.

#### China's "golden age" investment thesis
- **PRIMARY**: Li Lu argues China has crossed the Lewis Turning Point and entered the "golden age" (stage 2) — a self-reinforcing cycle of rising wages, consumption, savings, and investment. "Investment opportunities are still extremely rich and excellent" (投资机会仍然非常丰富、优异).
- **PRIMARY**: China's path from $10,000 to $20,000 per capita GDP "does not require the most advanced technology, only the spread of coastal city living standards to the interior" — driven by the "neighbor effect."
- This is Li Lu making a macro-level investment case for China as a market.

#### Policy regime determines investment environment
- **PRIMARY**: Different economic stages require different policy tools. Fiscal policy dominates in stage 1 (early industrialization), monetary policy in stage 2 (golden age), fiscal policy again in stage 3 (being-chased). Investors in the wrong stage using the wrong framework will misread the environment.
- Li Lu criticizes China's government for still using stage-1 administrative/fiscal tools when the economy has entered stage 2, where market forces and monetary policy should dominate — harming private enterprise confidence.

#### Zero/negative interest rates and asset pricing
- **PRIMARY**: Li Lu notes that QE created extraordinary monetary conditions — US excess reserves at 12.5x required, Japan at 32.5x — yet growth remained minimal. The main consequence: near-zero or negative interest rates and elevated asset prices across stocks, bonds, and real estate. "All asset prices... are at historical highs."
- This is a warning about valuation regime: asset prices inflated by monetary policy rather than fundamentals.

#### Private sector behavioral shift as analytical framework
- **PRIMARY**: The Koo framework distinguishes four economic states based on presence/absence of savers and borrowers. Li Lu adopts this as an analytical lens. When borrowers are absent (Japan post-1990, Europe post-2008), traditional economic predictions fail. For investors: corporate behavior in these regimes (prioritizing debt paydown over growth) fundamentally changes what "cheap" means.

## Cross-source patterns (echoes across both sources + earlier research files)

### 1. Investigative depth as edge
The 2006 lecture's insistence on investigative journalism (visit communities, read court filings, inspect properties) is the practical execution of what appears across Li Lu's writings as "knowledge-based investing." The edge comes not from models but from doing work others refuse to do.

### 2. Few insights, massive conviction
The 2006 lecture explicitly quantifies this: "five or ten insights" in a lifetime, concentration at 30-60%+ of portfolio. This echoes the Munger-school philosophy visible in earlier sources — the antithesis of diversification as risk management.

### 3. Business owner identity as foundation
Both the three pillars (2006) and the macro framework (2019) trace back to the same root: think like an owner. In 2006, this means margin of safety and long holding. In 2019, it means understanding when entire economies shift from profit-maximization to debt-minimization — the owner's perspective applied at macro scale.

### 4. Regime awareness
The 2019 review's developmental-stage framework (pre-Lewis, golden age, being-chased) is the macro extension of the 2006 lecture's "why does this opportunity exist?" checklist item. Li Lu insists on understanding the structural reason behind cheapness — whether at the company level (Timberland's Asia fear) or the economy level (China's stage-2 dynamics).

### 5. Anti-shorting conviction
The 2006 lecture provides the most explicit anti-shorting statement in the Li Lu corpus: it changes your mentality, forces trading, destroys insight-based investing. This is consistent with the long-only, owner-operator philosophy found across all sources.

### 6. China as investment opportunity
The 2019 essay provides the macro-intellectual framework for what is visible in Li Lu's portfolio: heavy China exposure. The "golden age" thesis — self-reinforcing wage-consumption-savings-investment cycle with decades of runway — is the structural argument behind concentrated China bets.

### 7. Compounding knowledge as the real edge
2006: "This process had a cumulative effect." Learning compounds. Biology from wife, history, physics — everything feeds investing. This is the intellectual framework that makes "five or ten insights in a lifetime" possible: decades of cross-disciplinary study create pattern recognition that others cannot replicate.
