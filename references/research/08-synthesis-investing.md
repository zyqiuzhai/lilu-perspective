# 08 — Investing Framework Synthesis

Synthesized from research files 01-06 and primary source transcripts (2013 G&D, 2015 Peking U, 2021 Greenwald, 2024 Peking U). Investing-only lens: civilization theory, geopolitics, and macro-history filtered out except where they directly generate an investing conclusion.

## 2.1 Mental Models (5 validated)

### Model 1: The Compounding Ownership Model
**One-line:** Stocks are fractional ownership of compounding businesses, not tradable paper — and compounding is the single most powerful force in investing.

**Triple validation:**
- Cross-domain recurrence: YES. Appears in (a) stock evaluation — "a stock is not just a tradable piece of paper... it represents a share of the company" [01, 05]; (b) portfolio construction — hold through 50% drawdowns because the business compounds regardless of price [05: BYD held 22 years through six 50%+ drawdowns]; (c) sell discipline — selling destroys the tax-free compounding ("borrowings from the government interest-free and legally" [02]).
- Generative power: YES. Predicts he would oppose frequent rebalancing, tax-loss harvesting, and any strategy that interrupts compounding. Predicts he would favor permanent-capital structures over hedge fund lockups.
- Exclusivity: PARTIAL. Buffett/Munger share this, but Li Lu's distinctive move is extending compounding from financial returns to a civilizational force — the 16,000-year hockey stick chart where compound growth defines modernity. This gives him unusually deep conviction in the model because he sees it as a law of civilization, not just a portfolio technique.

**Evidence:**
- "This can be explained mathematically by the magical powers of compounding, and indicates that the economic pattern of sustained, long-term compounding growth is a modern phenomenon." [01: 2015 Peking U lecture]
- "If you own them through the ups and downs, your return roughly approximates the actual business return to the capital we invested in the business itself. Over the long time the two tend to converge closely." [Greenwald 2021 transcript]
- Jeremy Siegel's 200-year data ($1 in stocks in 1802 becomes $1.03M vs. cash becoming $0.05) is his go-to empirical proof, cited in every major speech. [01, 03]

**Application method:** When evaluating any investment, first ask: "Is this a business that can compound value for 10+ years?" If no, it fails at the threshold. If yes, holding period should be measured in decades, not quarters.

**Failure conditions:** Breaks down when the business itself stops compounding (competitive advantage erodes), when leverage forces premature liquidation, or when the holding period is structurally short (fund redemptions). Li Lu himself acknowledges the last: Himalaya sometimes must sell into redemptions precisely because he refuses leverage [05].

**Confidence: HIGH** (3+ sources, 01/02/05)

### Model 2: The Four-Quadrant Epistemology
**One-line:** All investing knowledge falls into four buckets — what you know, what you don't know, what you don't need to know, and what you don't know you don't know — and survival depends on sorting correctly *before* the crisis.

**Triple validation:**
- Cross-domain recurrence: YES. Appears in (a) stock evaluation — defines circle of competence boundaries [01, 02]; (b) crisis management — "People freeze because they were not intellectually honest before. They never quite distinguished certain issues or questions and put them into the appropriate basket" [02: G&D 2013]; (c) portfolio construction — what goes in the "too hard" pile determines position sizing by exclusion [02].
- Generative power: YES. Predicts his refusal to opine on real estate, market levels, energy winners ("I put that in the 'too hard' basket. I also put that in the basket of 'I know I don't have to know'" [02]). Predicts he would say most AI investments are "too hard" for most investors.
- Exclusivity: YES. The four-part formulation is distinctively his. Graham had margin of safety; Munger had the three-bucket sort (yes/no/too hard). Li Lu adds the crucial third and fourth quadrants — "know what you don't need to know" and "you don't know that you don't know" — which are epistemic claims about the *structure of ignorance*, not just about humility.

**Evidence:**
- "The most important thing in our business is intellectual honesty. What I mean is four different things: know what you know, know what you don't know, know what you don't have to know, and realize that there is always a possibility that 'you don't know that you don't know.'" [02: G&D 2013, verbatim]
- Munger's test imported as the operational check: "I'm never entitled to have a view, until I can find the smartest people in the world who take the other side of that view and I can argue better for the opposition than they do." [02: Greenwald 2021]

**Application method:** Before committing capital, explicitly categorize each key question about the investment into one of the four quadrants. If the critical questions are in quadrants 2-4, do not invest. In a crisis, re-sort — the question is whether previously "known" things have moved to "don't know" territory.

**Failure conditions:** The model assumes you can accurately self-assess what you know, which is itself subject to Dunning-Kruger effects. Li Lu's partial answer is Munger's steelman test — but this requires access to smart opposing thinkers, which is not always available.

**Confidence: HIGH** (3+ sources, 01/02/03)

### Model 3: The Owner-Operator Lens
**One-line:** Evaluate every investment as if a dead uncle left you 100% of the company — this forces you to think about the business, not the stock.

**Triple validation:**
- Cross-domain recurrence: YES. Appears in (a) stock evaluation — "Imagine that one of your unknown uncle's died and he handed over 100% ownership of the company to you. That's the business you're going to study." [Greenwald 2021]; (b) management assessment — "The management team tends to be vested in their own biases and maybe doesn't look at the business as objectively rational as you do" [Greenwald 2021]; (c) crisis behavior — if you own 100%, a 50% stock drop is irrelevant to your assessment of the business.
- Generative power: YES. Predicts he would disfavor momentum strategies, technical analysis, macro-timing, or any approach that treats stocks as abstractions rather than businesses. Predicts his preference for studying one company deeply over screening thousands.
- Exclusivity: PARTIAL. This is Buffett's core insight too, but Li Lu's distinctive application is making it a *pedagogical method* — the thought experiment he assigns students as homework — and combining it with the specialist-generalist duality: "You always want to be a generalist in terms of being a student of business... By the time you invest into the companies you better become a true specialist to the point where you know hopefully better than anybody in the world including the management team." [Greenwald 2021]

**Evidence:**
- BYD as lived example: Li Lu knew Wang Chuanfu's engineering capability, factory operations, and competitive dynamics at a level exceeding most analysts. He could hold through 80%+ drawdowns because he understood the business as an owner would [05].
- "We insist on knowing a particular business inside and out, to the point where we are able to predict the outcome in the next ten years." [Greenwald 2021]

**Application method:** For any prospective investment, study it as if you will never be able to sell — you must understand the cash flows, competitive dynamics, management quality, and terminal value as a permanent owner. If you cannot hold with confidence through a 50% drawdown, you do not understand it well enough to own it.

**Failure conditions:** This model biases toward businesses the investor can personally understand, creating a structural blind spot for complex or rapidly changing industries. Li Lu explicitly acknowledges this by filing them under "too hard."

**Confidence: HIGH** (3+ sources, 01/02/05)

### Model 4: The Competitive Fortress with Dynamic Moat
**One-line:** Great businesses are not static fortresses — they are organizations whose culture and capital allocation ability allow them to *rebuild* the moat as conditions change.

**Triple validation:**
- Cross-domain recurrence: YES. Appears in (a) stock evaluation — looking for "above average return on invested capital" that is sustained because the company "can fend off competitors" [Greenwald 2021]; (b) management assessment — capital allocation is the crucial management skill: "Charlie and Warren skillfully took the last bit of the cash flow and invested it into other businesses and companies on the right side of the trajectory" [Greenwald 2021]; (c) his own portfolio evolution — moved from static cheap assets (Graham cigar butts) to dynamic compounders (BYD, Alphabet).
- Generative power: YES. Predicts he would prefer a company with a culture of adaptation (BYD as "learning machine") over one with a static moat (e.g., a regulated utility). Predicts he would value founder-led companies where capital allocation is centralized.
- Exclusivity: YES. This is distinctively Li Lu's synthesis. Graham valued cheapness. Buffett valued static moats. Munger valued quality at fair price. Li Lu's specific contribution is the emphasis on *dynamic* moats sustained by management culture, not just structural advantages. He explicitly pushed back on Greenwald's quantitative moat framework ("125-year moat" calculation): "A little bit of that and more... The most interesting aspect of businesses is that the only constant is the constant change." [Greenwald 2021]

**Evidence:**
- BYD moved from batteries to phones to cars to energy storage — each pivot rebuilt the moat via engineering culture. "This guy [Wang Chuanfu]... solved a whole bunch of different problems. So you have to admit the record is impressive." [02: G&D 2013]
- Berkshire as exemplar of capital reallocation: "it started out as a lousy textile business in New England, but Charlie and Warren skillfully took the last bit of the cash flow and invested it into other businesses." [Greenwald 2021]

**Application method:** Assess not just the current moat but the organization's ability to reallocate capital and rebuild advantages as industries shift. The question is: "What does this company look like in 10 years in the worst case scenario?" [Greenwald 2021]. If the culture can adapt, the moat is dynamic and potentially more durable than a static one.

**Failure conditions:** Overestimates the permanence of culture. Cultures erode, especially after founder departure. Li Lu's own BYD bet depends heavily on Wang Chuanfu's continued leadership.

**Confidence: HIGH** (3+ sources, 02/05/Greenwald transcript)

### Model 5: Wealth as Relative Purchasing Power
**One-line:** Wealth is not an absolute number but your share of purchasing power in the economy you consume in — invest accordingly in the most dynamic companies in the most vibrant economies.

**Triple validation:**
- Cross-domain recurrence: YES. Appears in (a) asset allocation — why you must hold equities (all other assets lose purchasing-power share over time; Siegel's cash → $0.05 example) [01]; (b) geographic allocation — "fish where the fish are" means finding the economies with the fastest compound growth, not the "safest" [01: 2024]; (c) sell discipline — the British noble with castles illustrates how static wealth shrinks relative to compound growth [01: 2024].
- Generative power: YES. Predicts his China exposure (fastest-growing major economy during his career). Predicts he would oppose gold, bonds-as-core-allocation, and inflation-hedging strategies that don't participate in real growth.
- Exclusivity: YES. This is Li Lu's self-described original contribution — his "sixth principle of value investing," explicitly claimed as his own addition to the Graham-Buffett-Munger lineage. "This principle is also the experience and contribution based on our practice at Himalaya Capital over the past thirty years." [01: 2024]

**Evidence:**
- "The essence of wealth is the proportion of purchasing power in the economy, and the goal of value investing is to hold shares of the most dynamic companies in the most vibrant economies, thereby preserving and growing wealth." [01: 2024 Peking U]
- The "ten-thousand-yuan households" (万元户) example: families that were rich in 1980s China became average within a decade because the economy grew faster than their static savings [01: 2024].
- British nobility with inherited castles as illustration of wealth erosion through non-participation in compounding [01: 2024].

**Application method:** When constructing a portfolio, the question is not "Will this asset preserve nominal value?" but "Will my share of total economic purchasing power grow?" This reframes the entire portfolio toward ownership of the most productive enterprises in the most dynamic economic environments.

**Failure conditions:** Requires a macro judgment about *which* economies are "most vibrant" — which contradicts Li Lu's own "don't do macro" advice. Also assumes modernization continues indefinitely; a genuine civilizational reversal (which Li Lu considers possible but improbable) would break the model.

**Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH** (2 sources, 01/06; the 2024 speech is the primary articulation; less cross-validated than models 1-4 because it is newer)

## 2.2 Decision Heuristics (9 total)

### Buy Heuristics

**H1: "Cheap is how you start; great-and-cheap is where you evolve"**
- If a business is statistically cheap on the balance sheet (Graham cigar butt), it is a valid starting point. But if a business has a durable competitive advantage AND is available at a discount to intrinsic value, it is the higher-order opportunity.
- Example: Li Lu's own career arc — "I started out looking for cheap securities... over time, I morphed into finding strong businesses at bargain prices." [01: G&D 2013]
- Source: 01, 02, 05. Confidence: HIGH.

**H2: "If you can't predict the worst case in 10 years, you don't understand it"**
- If you cannot articulate what the business looks like in the worst-case scenario over the next decade with high confidence, the investment fails the circle-of-competence test.
- Example: "At least I want to know what the business looks like in 10 years in the worst case scenario." [Greenwald 2021]
- Source: 02, Greenwald transcript. Confidence: HIGH.

**H3: "Buy more as they go down — if you truly understand"**
- If a stock you own drops 50%, the correct response is to buy more — but only if your understanding is genuine. If the drop makes you anxious rather than excited, you did not understand the business well enough.
- Example: BYD held through six 50%+ drawdowns; he bought more each time. "Because we understand them and they are great businesses we buy a lot more as they go down." [Greenwald 2021]
- Source: 02, 05. Confidence: HIGH.

**H4: "Fish in small lakes, not the biggest one"**
- If an investing opportunity is in a niche where few sophisticated competitors operate, it offers better odds than well-trawled waters. "Minnesota has over ten thousand lakes, but we don't need to fish in the largest one." [01: 2024, Munger's fishing-guide story]
- Source: 01, 03. Confidence: HIGH.

### Sell Heuristics

**H5: The Three (later Four) Conditions for Selling**
- Sell if: (1) you made a mistake in your original analysis — sell immediately, no pride; (2) valuation reaches extreme bubble territory; (3) you find a materially better opportunity (opportunity cost). (4) Client redemptions force the sale, because Himalaya refuses to use leverage [05: 2024 Q&A].
- "Those three conditions... That's why I almost never sell." [01: G&D 2013; restated 2024]
- Source: 01, 02, 05. Confidence: HIGH.

### Position Sizing Heuristic

**H6: "Extreme concentration follows extreme understanding"**
- If you truly understand a business at ownership depth, allocate heavily. If you merely think it is likely to go up, allocate nothing. The middle ground — moderate conviction, moderate position — is avoided.
- Evidence: Himalaya's US book: 9 holdings, top 5 = ~87% of AUM. Alphabet alone = ~44% [05]. Li Lu: "In my 30 years of investing, I've only encountered such opportunities a few times." [05: 2024]
- Source: 05, 04. Confidence: HIGH.

### "Too Hard" Pile Heuristic

**H7: "If it requires macro prediction, it goes in the too-hard pile"**
- If an investment thesis depends on predicting interest rates, commodity prices, currency movements, real estate cycles, or market levels, file it under "too hard" and "don't need to know."
- Example: On Chinese real estate — "I put that in the 'too hard' basket. I also put it in the basket of 'I know I don't have to know.'" On market valuation — "I only think about it when things go to an extreme." [02: G&D 2013]
- Source: 01, 02. Confidence: HIGH.

### Wait vs. Act Heuristic

**H8: "Study for years, then act in minutes"**
- If you have been studying a company for years and it enters your strike zone, act decisively. The preparation happens over long periods; the action happens fast.
- "Most of the time we don't have an answer and we keep at it, sometimes for years before we really understand it. After that we still have to wait for the price to come to our striking zone." [Greenwald 2021]
- Source: 02, Greenwald transcript. Confidence: HIGH.

### Anti-Heuristic (what to never do)

**H9: "If the payoff is asymmetrically against you, don't play"**
- If a strategy has limited upside and unlimited downside (shorting), or if it "screws up your mind" by diverting attention from long-term compounding, refuse the game entirely.
- Example: Quitting shorting in ~2004. "On the long side, you have 100% downside but unlimited upside. On the short side, you have 100% upside and unlimited downside... Lastly, it screws up your mind." [01: G&D 2013]
- Source: 01, 02, 05. Confidence: HIGH.

## 2.3 Expression DNA (Investing Context)

### Teaching Voice
Li Lu's investing voice is that of a **Confucian teacher crossed with a Graham-Dodd practitioner**. He builds arguments from first principles using numbered lists ("There are only 4 ideas... Let me repeat these six basic principles..."), defines terms before arguing ("What is fiduciary duty? What is wealth?"), and closes with moral exhortation ("Be brave and soldier on. This path is a beautiful one.") [03]

He is **assertive on principles, hedging on specifics**. When discussing the core framework, he uses absolute language: "There is only one philosophy and strategy... that can bring reliable, safe and outstanding returns: value investing." When discussing specific investments or predictions, he shifts to probability language: "I think there is a high probability that..." / "Nothing is 100%, but if you always swing when you have an overwhelmingly better edge..." [03]

### Certainty Patterns
- **Absolute certainty** on: the validity of value investing, the power of compounding, the importance of intellectual honesty, fiduciary duty as non-negotiable.
- **Probabilistic language** on: specific company outcomes, market timing, macro forecasts, which companies will win.
- **Refusal to engage** on: current holdings (except BYD), market levels, macro predictions, relative performance vs. peers.

### Signature Analogies for Investing Ideas
When explaining investing concepts, he reaches for:
1. **The empty road** — "On this wide open main path of investing you find very little traffic and wonder where everybody is." [01: 2015] Value investing is the uncrowded correct path; shortcuts are congested and lead nowhere.
2. **The dead uncle** — "Imagine that one of your unknown uncle's died and he handed over 100% ownership of the company to you." [Greenwald 2021] Forces owner mentality.
3. **Mr. Market as trap for the dishonest** — "The financial markets seem to be there to catch human weaknesses. If you don't understand something, and just pretend to, you will be busted at some point." [Greenwald 2021]
4. **Fishing** — "There are two rules to fishing: the first is to fish where the fish are, and the second is to never forget the first rule." [01: 2024] Circle of competence and opportunity selection.
5. **Tax-deferred compounding as interest-free government loan** — unrealized gains are "borrowings from the government interest-free and legally." [02: G&D 2013]
6. **The inoculation** — "Value investing is like an inoculation — either it takes or it doesn't." [02: G&D 2013, attributed to Buffett] Some people will never convert.
7. **The crisis as moral X-ray** — "People freeze because they were not intellectually honest before." [02: G&D 2013] Crises reveal pre-existing epistemic failures, not new problems.

### Vocabulary He Avoids in Investing Context
He does not use: alpha, beta, Sharpe ratio, factor, momentum, breakout, resistance, disruptive, game-changing. He avoids all quantitative finance jargon and all hype vocabulary. His investing lexicon is deliberately pre-modern-finance: margin of safety, circle of competence, intrinsic value, Mr. Market, compounding. [03]

## 2.4 Values & Anti-patterns (Investing)

### Core Investing Values (ranked by emphasis across sources)

1. **Intellectual honesty** — the foundational value. "The most important thing in our business is intellectual honesty." [02: G&D 2013]. Ranked first because it appears in every source, is the subject of his most quoted passage, and is the precondition for all other values in his framework. [01/02/03/04/05]

2. **Fiduciary duty** — treated as innate and unlearnable. "I tend to think that fiduciary duty is something innate, part of someone's DNA. Those born without cannot acquire it later by any means or through any effort." [01: 2015 Peking U]. He structures Himalaya to enforce this: zero management fee, 100% of personal capital in the fund, Buffett Partnership terms. [01/05]

3. **Long-term compounding** — both a technique and a moral commitment. Selling interrupts compounding; therefore, selling without strong cause is a failure of discipline. Patience is not just strategically optimal but ethically required when managing others' capital. [01/02/05]

4. **Skin in the game** — "I don't invest anything outside of the fund. I put all of my investment capital into my funds. So it's a true partnership." [01: G&D 2013]. Alignment of incentives is not optional but structural. [05]

5. **Win-win orientation** — investing should create value for all parties. His BYD narrative emphasizes that his investment helped BYD succeed, which helped Munger, which helped his LPs, which helped consumers. The Capital IQ exit is narrated as a win-win with operators. [02/05]

### Things He Explicitly Opposes

1. **Shorting** — "One of the worst mistakes I've made." Asymmetric downside, fraud can outlast solvency, and it "screws up your mind" by making you root against human progress. [01/02/05]

2. **Leverage** — Himalaya refuses to borrow, ever. Li Lu accepts the cost of forced selling into redemptions rather than using leverage. "We don't borrow." [05: 2024 Q&A]

3. **Management fees without performance** — the 2-and-20 hedge fund model is described with unmistakable contempt. His own structure: zero management fee, 6% hurdle, 25% carry over the hurdle. [01: G&D 2013; 03]

4. **Macro forecasting as investment method** — "I put that in the 'too hard' basket." He treats macro as defensive context (understand tailwinds/headwinds) but explicitly opposes making investment decisions based on macro predictions. [02]

5. **Index-hugging / benchmark-relative investing** — never mentioned approvingly. His 9-holding, 87%-top-5 portfolio is structurally anti-benchmark. [05]

6. **Fast trading / speculation** — "Investors take shortcuts because the right and main path takes too long." The entire momentum/speculation complex is dismissed as 歧路 (heterodox path). [01: 2015]

7. **Motivated reasoning / "where you sit determines how you think"** — the single epistemic hazard he warns about most frequently. Asset managers who let their fee structure determine their market views are his primary negative example. [01/03]

### Internal Tensions (investing-specific)

**Tension 1: "Ignore macro" yet performs extensive macro analysis.**
He tells students: "the macro environment is what we must accept... the micro level is where we can make a difference." [01: 2024] Yet his 2024 Peking University keynote devotes roughly 75% of its length to macro-political analysis of China's middle-income trap, Hong Kong's role, and US-China relations. His 2015 lecture is substantially a macro-civilizational argument. His resolution: macro understanding is *defensive* (you need it to hold through drawdowns and to understand tailwinds) but should never drive *buy/sell decisions*. The tension is real but the distinction between "macro as context" and "macro as signal" may be thinner in practice than in theory. [01/02/05. Confidence in the tension: HIGH.]

**Tension 2: "Intellectual humility" yet extreme concentration.**
His rhetoric emphasizes "know what you don't know" and the importance of the "too hard" pile. His portfolio is among the most concentrated in institutional investing — ~44% in one company (Alphabet), ~87% in five positions. [04/05] His resolution: concentration IS the expression of humility — he only bets on the tiny number of things he truly understands, and passes on everything else. The tension is interpretive: outsiders see concentration as confidence; he frames it as the logical output of honest self-assessment. [04/05. Confidence: HIGH.]

**Tension 3: "Fiduciary duty is innate DNA" yet teaches value investing for a decade.**
He claims the key ethical trait cannot be acquired: "Those born without cannot acquire it later by any means or through any effort." [01: 2015] Yet he runs a decade-long value investing course at Peking University and repeatedly offers pedagogical advice to students. His implicit resolution: the *technique* of value investing is teachable; the *character* required to practice it honestly is not. He is teaching methodology to those who already have the DNA, while warning those who lack it to find another profession. [01. Confidence: MEDIUM — the resolution is implied, not stated.]

**Tension 4: "Circle of competence is small" yet invests across many industries.**
His portfolio spans Chinese autos/batteries (BYD), US search/advertising (Alphabet), US banking (BAC), Chinese e-commerce (PDD), and US conglomerates (Berkshire). [05] His resolution: the circle is defined not by industry but by depth of understanding of specific businesses. "You always want to be a generalist in terms of being a student of business... By the time you invest, you better become a true specialist." [Greenwald 2021] The circle grows as the investor grows. [02/05. Confidence: HIGH.]

## 2.5 Intellectual Lineage (Investing)

### Graham -> Buffett -> Munger -> Li Lu Chain

**From Benjamin Graham, Li Lu took:**
- The three foundational principles: (1) stock as fractional ownership, (2) Mr. Market serves you, doesn't guide you, (3) margin of safety. These are explicitly named as "Graham's three principles" in every major speech. [01]
- The cigar-butt approach as starting methodology — "I started out looking for cheap securities." [01: G&D 2013]
- The concept of crisis as opportunity rather than catastrophe.
- What he *left behind* from Graham: the purely statistical approach to cheapness, the diversified cigar-butt portfolio, and Graham's relative indifference to business quality.

**From Warren Buffett, Li Lu took:**
- The fourth principle: circle of competence — "know what you know, and know what you don't know." [01]
- The evolution from cheap-securities to quality-businesses: "Finding strong businesses at bargain prices." [01]
- The Buffett Partnership fee structure: zero management fee, 6% hurdle, 25% carry. Li Lu adopted this exact structure. [05: G&D 2013]
- The origin story itself: Li Lu's career began by attending a Buffett lecture at Columbia. "To my dismay there was no lunch. [laughs] There was just a guy with the name 'Buffett.'" [02: G&D 2013]
- What he *does differently* from Buffett: invests primarily in Asia/China (where Buffett has explicitly said he lacks competence), accepts concentration beyond even Berkshire's levels in the equity portfolio, and frames investing within a civilizational theory that Buffett has never articulated.

**From Charlie Munger, Li Lu took:**
- The fifth principle: "Fish where the fish are." [01: 2024]
- The three-bucket sort: yes, no, or too hard — "Most of the things fall in 'too hard.'" [02: G&D 2013]
- The steelman test: "I'm never entitled to have a view, until I can find the smartest people in the world who take the other side of that view and I can argue better for the opposition than they do." [02: Greenwald 2021]
- The emphasis on management quality and business culture over static moats.
- The temperamental model: "His attitude towards life and his ability to keep equanimity in a phase of turbulence and maintaining that rational composure, commonsense approach to all problems." [Greenwald 2021]
- The personal relationship: 20 years of Tuesday dinners, summers at Star Island, and eventually Munger committing his family capital. [02/05/06]
- What he *adds beyond* Munger: Munger was comfortable with shorting in his partnership era; Li Lu abandoned it entirely. Munger's civilizational interests were broad but never systematized into a framework; Li Lu built the Civilization 1.0/2.0/3.0 taxonomy. Munger was US-centric in practice; Li Lu is the bridge to China.

**Li Lu's own contributions to the lineage:**
- The sixth principle: "Wealth is the proportion of purchasing power in the economy... invest in the most dynamic companies in the most vibrant economies." [01: 2024] He explicitly claims this as his personal addition: "This principle is also the experience and contribution based on our practice at Himalaya Capital over the past thirty years."
- The four-quadrant epistemology (expanding Munger's three buckets into four distinct knowledge categories). [02]
- The emphasis on *dynamic* moats sustained by organizational culture, not static structural advantages. [Greenwald 2021]
- The civilizational grounding of compounding — treating the 300-year compound-growth phenomenon as a feature of modernity, not just of good businesses. [01]

**Who he disagrees with and why:**
- **Efficient Market Hypothesis / academic finance** (Fama, Markowitz): absent from his citations; treated as irrelevant or wrong. He does not even bother to argue against them — they are simply excluded from his intellectual universe. [01/03]
- **Greenwald on zero-sum markets**: "You are arguing a slightly different view, but I'm never going to argue against my professor. Let's just agree we disagree on that one." [Greenwald 2021] Li Lu believes compounding makes investing positive-sum in the long run; Greenwald argued it's "a hundred percent zero-sum."
- **Behavioral finance** (Kahneman/Thaler): also absent from his citations, but his framework implicitly incorporates their insights — he describes markets as driven by "greed and fear," "hard coded instincts," and the human tendency to be "excellent at rationalization but not at being rational." [Greenwald 2021] He uses the insights without crediting the field.

## 2.6 Honest Boundaries

### What This Investing Framework Cannot Do

1. **It cannot generate short-term returns.** The framework is designed for decade-plus holding periods. Anyone needing to show quarterly performance, beat a benchmark annually, or justify positions to an impatient board will find it structurally incompatible. Li Lu himself says "the biggest problem is it takes too long." [01: 2015]

2. **It cannot be applied at scale by many people simultaneously.** The framework requires deep, proprietary understanding of specific businesses. If many investors adopt the same owner-mentality approach to the same businesses, the informational edge disappears. Li Lu is explicit: "Once you achieve a certain notoriety in a certain field, the people tend to really copy that. This is not the behavior we want to encourage." [Greenwald 2021] Value investing is self-limiting — the more practitioners, the fewer opportunities.

3. **It cannot handle rapidly changing industries well.** Li Lu files most technology questions under "too hard" and acknowledges that "the pace of change" makes prediction difficult in tech. [Greenwald 2021] His tech investments (Alphabet, PDD) are in companies with established dominance, not early-stage bets. The framework is better suited to durable businesses than to frontier innovation.

4. **It requires temperament that most people lack.** "The hallmark of a good investor is if you can watch your portfolio go down 50% and not be affected at all." [Greenwald 2021] Li Lu treats this as a filter, not a skill that can be developed. If you cannot hold through severe drawdowns, the framework will not work for you — and he believes most people cannot. "Value investing is like an inoculation — either it takes or it doesn't." [02]

5. **It depends on a civilizational optimism that may not hold.** The framework assumes the 300-year trend of compound economic growth continues. Li Lu acknowledges this assumption explicitly but considers the probability of reversal "very low." [01: 2015] In a genuine global civilizational collapse, the framework fails. More realistically, in a prolonged period of economic stagnation (Japan 1990-2020), the framework's returns compress severely — though Li Lu would argue that even in Japan, carefully selected businesses compounded.

6. **It cannot separate the macro from the micro as cleanly as it claims.** Li Lu's own practice reveals that macro understanding deeply shapes his investment choices (China vs. Europe; consumer-driven vs. export-driven economies; which economy is "most vibrant"). The stated framework says "ignore macro, focus on businesses." The practiced framework says "understand macro deeply, then find the best businesses within the best macro." This is not a fatal flaw, but it is a gap between the simplified teaching version and the actual method. [01/02/05]

7. **It offers no systematic risk management beyond stock selection.** There is no hedging, no portfolio insurance, no explicit drawdown limits, no systematic position-sizing formula. Risk management IS stock selection — if you chose well, you don't need hedging. If you chose badly, sell immediately (condition 1). This is elegant but binary: it works perfectly when the investor's judgment is correct and fails completely when it is wrong. The margin of safety at purchase is the only formal risk control.

8. **The track record is private and unverifiable.** Himalaya Capital is a private partnership with no audited public track record. The framework's claimed success rests on (a) Li Lu's own testimony, (b) Munger's public endorsements, and (c) partial 13F data for US positions only. An honest assessment must note that the framework's real-world validation cannot be independently confirmed to the standard that, say, Berkshire's public returns can be. [04/05]
