# 02 — Long Conversations & Spontaneous Thinking

Primary sources used:
- [PRIMARY] Greenwald-Li Lu dialogue, 13th Columbia China Business Conf., April 10 2021 (full transcript, docx)
- [PRIMARY] Graham & Doddsville Q&A interview, Spring 2013, Columbia Business School student newsletter
- [PRIMARY] Li Lu lecture + implicit Q&A at Peking University Guanghua, Oct 23 2015 ("The Prospect of Value Investing in China")

## How he responds to being challenged

**Pattern 1 — Concedes the procedural point but refuses to abandon the substance.** When Greenwald pushes him to share a specific company example of crisis-resilience (after Li Lu had said "I do [have examples], but we normally don't talk about specific companies"), Greenwald presses again: "But could you share maybe a historical experience of that, so that the students in the audience have a sense of what to look for?" Li Lu answers:
> "Bruce, we tried this multiple times in your class. There is a simple logical reason we don't do that. Once you achieve a certain notoriety in a certain field, the people tend to really copy that. This is not the behavior we want to encourage. Instead of giving people fish, it is much better to teach them how to fish."
He redirects with an idiom rather than escalating — polite, firm, self-referential ("we tried this multiple times"). [PRIMARY: Greenwald transcript]

**Pattern 2 — Publicly disagreeing with a mentor/professor without conceding.** When Greenwald insists investing is "a hundred percent zero-sum," Li Lu pushes back once, then de-escalates:
> Li Lu: "There is some zero-sum aspect, but not always."
> Bruce: "It's a hundred percent zero-sum. The average return to all investors in any asset class… is the average return to all those assets."
> Li Lu: "You are arguing a slightly different view, but I'm never going to argue against my professor. Let's just agree we disagree on that one."
Note the pattern: he states his actual view, names the disagreement explicitly, then uses deference as an exit. He does NOT fold his view. [PRIMARY: Greenwald]

**Pattern 3 — Reframes "what is different about you?" questions by refusing to psychoanalyze competitors.** Greenwald asks three times in different forms how Li Lu does things "differently from other investors." Each time Li Lu redirects from competitive-positioning framing to process framing:
> "I don't spend my time studying a hundred people doing that. We spend most of our time studying industries and specific companies."
He will not answer questions shaped around relative comparison to peers. [PRIMARY: Greenwald]

**Pattern 4 — When asked to predict macro extremes, he files the question into "too hard."** To G&D on whether Chinese real estate was in a bubble: "I put that in the 'too hard' basket. I also put in the basket of 'I know I don't have to know.' It certainly is 'I don't know,' but I also know that I don't have to know! I don't want those things to worry me." On market valuation today: "I also put that into 'too hard' and 'I know I don't have to know.' I only think about it when things go to an extreme." [PRIMARY: G&D 2013]

## Spontaneous analogies and metaphors

- **Buffett's value-investing talk = "a punch in my eyes" / "a light switched on"** — the physical-jolt metaphor for first exposure. [G&D 2013]
- **"Value investing is like an inoculation — either it takes or it doesn't"** (attributed to Buffett, adopted as his own). [G&D 2013]
- **Holding unrealized gains as "borrowings from the government interest-free and legally"** — spontaneous reframe of deferred cap-gains tax as cheap leverage, used to explain why selling destroys ROE. [G&D 2013]
- **Shorting: "100% upside, unlimited downside… I do not like that math. The best short has some element of fraud. However, a fraud can be perpetrated for a long time."** — reaches for the asymmetry framing, then adds: "Shorts just grab your mind and take away from the concentrated effort." [G&D 2013]
- **"Charlie has three buckets: yes, no, or too hard. Most things fall in 'too hard.'"** — reaches for this whenever pushed about industries or forecasts. [G&D 2013, reused in 2021 Greenwald and 2015 Peking]
- **"The hallmark of a good investor is if you can watch your portfolio go down 50% and not be affected at all."** [Greenwald 2021]
- **"The financial markets seem to be there to catch human weaknesses… It was almost designed that way to catch you."** — recurring metaphor of market-as-trap-for-the-dishonest. Used in both 2021 and 2015. [Greenwald; Peking]
- **"Your unknown uncle died and handed you 100% of the company"** — the owner-mentality thought experiment he uses with students. [Greenwald]
- **"1+1>2 and 1+1>4"** — his signature formula for free trade (goods) vs. free exchange of ideas. Deployed spontaneously in Peking Q&A. [Peking 2015]
- **Civilization 1.0 / 2.0 / 3.0** — his go-to scaffolding when asked any big "is China different?" question. [Peking 2015; alluded to in Greenwald]
- **BYD as "a learning machine"** — when pushed on car quality. [G&D 2013]
- **"Every time they bill it as 'once in a century,' except these major events happen every five years in my case. [laughs]"** — his reflex joke when asked about crisis navigation. Used verbatim in both 2013 and 2021. [G&D; Greenwald]
- **"In the long run we are all dead"** (Keynes) — he reaches for this when acknowledging the gap between theory and human investment horizons. [Peking]

## Public mind-changes / view updates

- **Gave up shorting around 2004** ("nine years ago" from 2013). He frames this as an explicit update: "Shorting was one of the worst mistakes I've made… It was a mistake on my part. I shorted for a couple of years." He lists three reasons (asymmetric math, fraud can be perpetrated longer than you can stay solvent, it "screws up your mind") and adds a fourth retrospectively (the economy compounds upward for 300 years). The update is structural, not circumstantial. [G&D 2013]
- **Evolved from Graham cigar-butts to quality compounders:** "I started out looking for cheap securities… Over time I morphed into finding strong businesses at bargain prices. I still have a streak in me that favors finding really cheap securities — I just can't help it!" He frames the evolution as personal-growth, not a repudiation. [G&D 2013; repeated almost verbatim to Greenwald in 2021]
- **Moved away from venture/building companies toward public markets** because "the venture side is hard to scale." Notably he doesn't say it was wrong — he says the win-win satisfaction was real but the effort didn't scale. [G&D 2013]

## Questions he refuses or deflects

1. **Specific current portfolio companies.** "We normally don't talk about specific companies." Reason: copycats / wants to teach fishing not give fish. [Greenwald]
2. **Predicting stock indices / bull-bear tops.** "You won't casually state 5000 point is too low, that a bull market is imminent, or that 4000 point is a bottoming out. You will know that such pronouncements are outside of your circle of competence." [Peking 2015]
3. **Macro calls on real estate, overall equity levels, energy revolution winners.** Files under "too hard" + "don't have to know." Reason given: macro trends matter only as tailwind-vs-headwind check. [G&D 2013]
4. **Favorite Munger quote.** Deflects with "Oh, there are so many" and redirects to ethos rather than picking one. [G&D 2013]
5. **Warren Buffett personal closeness.** Gently corrects the implicit framing — says he admires Buffett but knows him mainly through writings, not personally, because "Warren is in Omaha." [G&D 2013]
6. **Which EV company will win.** "Well I backed one, so I like my money to speak for itself. We're not going to be the only one, there's going to be a few." [Greenwald]

## Dialogue with Munger

Li Lu describes Munger as "mentor, teacher, partner, and friend, all in one," with "dinner every Tuesday night" up until the pandemic (stated in 2021). What's distinctive:
- He frames the learning as primarily **non-investment**: "The greatest influence from Charlie however, was really beyond investing. He was more of a role model and the way he conducts himself in real life." [Greenwald 2021]
- The role-model framing is theorized: "For every profession one should have role models in life. We find them among the eminent dead, because it is safer. It is risky to pick a role model that is still alive, because they have the risk of disappointing you. I got lucky that my role model never failed and instead continues to inspire me, right into his current 97th year." [Greenwald 2021]
- He imports Munger's catchphrases verbatim and labels them as such: the "three buckets" (yes/no/too hard), and Munger's epistemic rule: **"I'm never entitled to have a view, until I can find the smartest people in the world who take the other side of that view and I can argue better for the opposition than they do."** He quotes this repeatedly across interviews as the test of intellectual honesty. [G&D 2013; Greenwald 2021]
- On BYD, he notes "Charlie knows more about BYD than Warren" — an unusual willingness to point out a knowledge asymmetry inside the Buffett/Munger pair rather than flattening them. [G&D 2013]

## Dialogue with academics (Greenwald)

The 2021 Greenwald exchange is the cleanest evidence we have of how he thinks while being challenged by a peer-level academic he respects. Distinctive moves:

- **He opens by crediting Greenwald for his entry into the field** ("I got into this field after hearing Warren Buffet speaking at the first lecture of professor Greenwald's class… Thank you.") — sets a hierarchical but affectionate tone.
- **He refuses Greenwald's explicit framework import.** When Greenwald offers a very specific moat-quantification framework ("125 year moat" math for soft drinks; explicit economies-of-scale × customer-captivity two-dimensional test), Li Lu replies "A little bit of that and more," then redirects to dynamic/change arguments: "The most interesting aspect of businesses is that the only constant is the constant change." He absorbs Greenwald's vocabulary ("scale economy") but pivots to management-and-culture as the real moat. He will not be pinned to Greenwald's mental model.
- **He ends the zero-sum argument with deference plus a line in the sand.** (See Pattern 2 above.) The phrase "I'm never going to argue against my professor" is notable because the sentence immediately preceding it contradicts the professor.
- **On China slowdown data (Greenwald presses him with hard trade data showing Chinese growth has slowed):** Li Lu does not dispute the data. He reframes: "It tells you that the characteristics of the Chinese economy has changed fundamentally" — and pivots from export-driven to consumer-driven. He accepts the factual jab and uses it as setup for his thesis.
- **On service economies (Greenwald presses that China's service sector is weak):** Li Lu again agrees ("You're right that at the current stage the service sector has yet to become as powerful… but that's really an amazing set of opportunities for the decades ahead"). Pattern: concede the present, reframe as opportunity for the future.

## Student Q&A patterns

From Peking University 2015 lecture (and the G&D student-newsletter Q&A, which functions similarly):

- **He reframes "how do I become a great investor?" as "do you have the DNA?"** His two cardinal principles are pushed hardest at students: (1) intellectual obligation to truth-seeking, (2) fiduciary duty. He says fiduciary duty is "innate, part of someone's DNA. Those born without cannot acquire it later by any means or through any effort." Remarkably strong — he tells students who lack it "please heed my advice and don't join this profession."
- **He teaches through one thought-experiment repeatedly:** study one business as if a dead uncle left you 100% of it. He reused this in Peking 2015 and the 2021 Greenwald dialogue.
- **He refuses shortcut questions.** When students want tips or current market views, he deflects with the "wide open main path vs. congested shortcut paths" metaphor — the right way is empty because it takes too long.
- **On advice for students who can't meet Buffett:** "If you do get a chance to meet Mr. Buffett, I'd run to it if I were you. I wouldn't even take an airplane; I would just run to Omaha! [laughs]" — spontaneous humor, then pivots to "study one business inside and out." [G&D 2013]

## Direct quotes showing real-time reasoning

On crises (near-verbatim in two different years, showing it's his default groove, not fresh thinking):
> "You know every time that that happens, they always bill it as 'once in a century,' except these major events happen every five years in my case. [laughs] What is interesting about crisis is that it puts your intellectual honesty to the test." [G&D 2013]

The four-part "intellectual honesty" framework, delivered live:
> "Know what you know, know what you don't know, know what you don't have to know, and realize that there is always a possibility that 'you don't know that you don't know.' Those four things are distinctly different. In a crisis, things emerge that test you on all four categories." [G&D 2013]

On why people freeze in crises (diagnostic rather than sympathetic):
> "People freeze because they were not intellectually honest before. They never quite distinguished certain issues or questions and put them into the appropriate basket." [G&D 2013]

On being a specialist vs. generalist — a live reframe:
> "You always want to be a generalist in terms of being a student of business… By the time you invest into the companies you better become a true specialist to the point where you know hopefully better than anybody in the world including the management team. The management team tends to be vested in their own biases and maybe doesn't look at the business as objectively rational as you do." [Greenwald 2021]

On human nature and markets (shows his anthropological framing in real time):
> "Humans as a product of evolution aren't run in a very rational way. We are excellent at rationalization, but we aren't good at being rational. We are wired by hard coded instincts looking for zero-sum, fast money and get scared when things go against us." [Greenwald 2021]

On his own luck (reflexive response to "what would you have done differently?"):
> "When I look back, I feel extraordinary luck and feel nothing but gratitude… I look back at my career and have nothing to regret." [Greenwald 2021]

Refusing to name a favorite Munger quote (shows how he deflects sentimental questions):
> "Oh, there are so many. We share a fundamental ethos about life and about approaching investing. So I learn more about how to conduct myself personally as much, if not more, than investing." [G&D 2013]
