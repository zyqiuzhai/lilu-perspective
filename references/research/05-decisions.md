## Signature decision: BYD (2002–present)

**What we can verify (PRIMARY, from Li Lu's own 2013 G&D interview and 2024 Peking University speech):**

- **Entry year: 2002.** In his December 2024 speech at Peking University, Li Lu states directly: *"Another example is BYD, which we've held for 22 years."* (2024 − 22 = 2002.) This is his own testimony, not a secondary retelling.
- **Holding period: 22+ years and counting as of late 2024.** Still held.
- **Drawdowns survived while holding:** *"During this time, its stock has dropped by more than 50% at least six times, once even by more than 80%."* (PRIMARY, 2024 speech.) This is the cleanest statement of his actual behavior under pressure that exists in the source base.
- **Fundamentals growth during hold:** *"BYD's sales grew from one billion yuan to nearly one trillion yuan"* during his holding period (PRIMARY, 2024).
- **Entry logic (PRIMARY, 2013 G&D):** "This guy [Wang Chuanfu], who is a really terrific engineer, started the business from just a $300,000 loan with no additional money until the IPO. He created a company with $8 billion in revenue and 170,000 employees... He solved a whole bunch of different problems. So you have to admit the record is impressive... At the time we invested, we had quite a bit of a margin of safety." Logic: founder quality + engineering culture as "learning machine" + margin of safety at entry price, *not* an EV-thesis bet.
- **Munger introduction and Berkshire investment (2008):** Li Lu introduced BYD to Charlie Munger, who then championed it inside Berkshire. Berkshire/MidAmerican took a ~10% stake in BYD in September 2008. Li Lu confirms his role in the 2024 speech: *"If we hadn't introduced a reputable investor like Berkshire in 2008, BYD's success would have faced more challenges."* (PRIMARY.) In the 2013 G&D interview he confirms Munger knew BYD better than Buffett did: *"Warren and Charlie have a great partnership and Charlie knows more about BYD than Warren."*
- **Why this is THE decision:** BYD is the trade that credentialed Li Lu with Munger. Munger has publicly called it his best investment idea from Li Lu. It is the single position that most defines his reputation and is the one he voluntarily chooses as his illustrative example when asked to defend the social value of value investors (2024 Q&A).

**What I cannot verify from primary sources in this file:**
- Exact entry price, exact position size in dollars at entry, or the percentage of the fund at entry. Himalaya was a private partnership in 2002 and did not file 13Fs (BYD is Hong Kong–listed and would not appear in 13F anyway). Any specific dollar figure floating around online is SECONDARY and I am not repeating it.

## Other known major positions (US equities only — Himalaya 13F)

**Source:** Himalaya Capital Management LLC, SEC Form 13F filings (PRIMARY, regulatory). 13F covers *only* US-listed long equity positions above reporting thresholds. It does NOT show: BYD (HK-listed), Kweichow Moutai (A-share), Postal Savings Bank of China (HK), or any other non-US holdings, short positions, or cash. So the 13F view is structurally incomplete.

**Portfolio trajectory from 13F history (Q4 2016 – Q4 2025):**

- **2016–2018 (early 13F period):** Chinese ADRs dominated the US-reported book — Baidu (BIDU), Sina (SINA), Weibo (WB), Alibaba (BABA).
- **2019–2021:** Rotated hard into US names. Core holdings became Micron (MU), Bank of America (BAC), Facebook/Meta (META), and Alphabet (GOOGL/GOOG).
- **2022–2025:** Concentrated further. By Q4 2025, 9 total holdings, ~$3.57B reported. Top 5:
  - Alphabet Class A — ~22.3%
  - Alphabet Class C — ~21.6%
  - Bank of America — ~16.1% (trimmed ~24.7% in latest quarter)
  - PDD Holdings (Pinduoduo ADR) — ~14.6%
  - Berkshire Hathaway Class B — ~12.6%
- **Portfolio value trajectory:** ~$112M (Q4 2016) → ~$3.57B (Q4 2025). ~32x over the decade. Note this conflates inflows, compounding, and markups; it is not a performance figure.

**Kweichow Moutai, Postal Savings Bank of China, and other China-listed holdings** are widely reported as Himalaya positions, but I cannot verify them from the primary sources in this task's whitelist (13F doesn't cover them, and Li Lu himself refuses to discuss holdings publicly — per the 2024 Q&A: *"I generally don't talk about holdings, but since everyone knows about our investment in BYD, let's use it as an example."*). ## Positions he exited — and why

**Shorting (exited ~2004).** PRIMARY, 2013 G&D: *"That change occurred nine years ago. Shorting was one of the worst mistakes I've made."* Three stated reasons: asymmetric payoff (100% up, unlimited down), frauds can outlast your solvency, and "it screws up your mind." A fourth reason added: the economy compounds upward, so shorts fight the base rate. Action-reveals-belief note: he exited a whole strategy after concluding it was incompatible with his temperament and with his concentrated long-term posture. Clean alignment of action and stated belief.

**Chinese ADRs (2016→2019).** 13F data shows Baidu, Sina, Weibo, Alibaba were all completely eliminated from the US-reported book by 2019. Possible interpretations: rotation into US-listed names where he had higher conviction; partial response to US-China regulatory risk on VIE structures. Li Lu has not publicly explained these exits in the sources available here. Do NOT fabricate a narrative.

**Micron and Meta.** Held and then exited in the 2022–2024 window per 13F. No primary public explanation.

**Capital IQ (venture, early 2000s).** PRIMARY, 2013 G&D: sold early, made money but "if we would have kept it, we would have been far richer!" He was the first investor. Described it as a lesson in selling winners too early but explicitly *not* regretted because he valued the win-win with operators. Reveals: he prefers win-win constructive capital to pure extraction.

## Decision to focus on China (and the US)

Li Lu's own description (PRIMARY, 2013 G&D): *"I don't have a preconceived notion about allocation. I let the opportunity dictate where I end up. I just happen to have more interest in Asia and the U.S., so that's where I end up. I do not feel that interest in Europe. I do not feel that in Africa."*

So this was not a formal "move capital to China" moment — it was temperamental and opportunity-driven, justified on circle-of-competence grounds. His circle of competence is defined by *personal interest*, not by geographic mandate. In 2024 the macro framing shifts: he now talks about holding "the most dynamic companies in the most vibrant economies" globally, language that is less China-centric than the 2013 version. The 13F evidence tracks this — US names became dominant in the US book from 2019 onward.

## Concentration and holding period (what the record actually shows)

- **Concentration (US book, 13F, PRIMARY):** 9 holdings, top 5 = ~87% of reported AUM as of Q4 2025. Top 2 (Alphabet A + C, same underlying company) = ~44%. This is a genuinely concentrated portfolio by any institutional standard.
- **Holding period (PRIMARY, Li Lu's own statement):** BYD = 22+ years and still held. In the 2024 Q&A he says finding a great-and-cheap company is *"a very rare opportunity. In my 30 years of investing, I've only encountered such opportunities a few times."* This implies the portfolio's best ideas are measured in decades, not quarters.
- **Sell discipline (PRIMARY, 2013 G&D and 2024 Q&A, consistent across 11 years):** sell on (1) mistake, (2) a materially better opportunity, (3) extreme/bubble overvaluation, and (4) client redemptions (2024 addition — because Himalaya refuses to borrow/use leverage).

## Accepting Munger family capital

The Munger family became an early and sustaining LP of Himalaya. This is widely reported and is a matter of public record via Munger's own statements at Daily Journal and Berkshire meetings over the years (PRIMARY in the sense of Munger saying it himself; I do not have those transcripts in the local source set, so flagging SECONDARY here).

**What this signaled (action, not words):**
- Munger does not give capital to people he hasn't independently validated. Accepting the capital was Li Lu's signal that he was willing to be accountable to a peer-level investor who would know whether he was thinking straight.
- Li Lu structurally eliminated agency conflict by adopting the original Buffett Partnership fee structure: zero management fee, 6% hurdle, 25% over the hurdle. PRIMARY, 2013 G&D: *"I don't invest anything outside of the fund. I put all of my investment capital into my funds. So it's a true partnership."* This is the decision that matches the relationship — he made it structurally impossible to get rich on fees rather than returns.

## Life decisions

- **Tiananmen 1989 → fleeing China.** Biographical fact. Li Lu was a student leader in the 1989 protests and subsequently left China. He describes his arrival in the US in the 2013 G&D: *"I was dirt poor. I did not choose to come here — I just ended up here because I had nowhere else to go, having just escaped from China after Tiananmen. I was in a new country where I didn't understand the language, didn't know anybody, and didn't have a penny to my name."* He wrote a memoir (*Moving the Mountain*) about the period. No editorializing.

- **Columbia Business School (and Columbia College, Columbia Law).** PRIMARY, G&D header bio: B.A. Columbia College, J.D. Columbia Law, M.B.A. Columbia Business School — he did all three simultaneously, an unusual credential stack. The decision that mattered more than the degree itself: attending a lecture by Warren Buffett at Columbia around 1993, which he describes as the lightswitch moment (PRIMARY, 2013 G&D). He went because a friend told him there'd be free food. The entire career is downstream of that one hour.

- **Choice of Munger as mentor.** PRIMARY, 2013: *"Charlie and I have some very close mutual friends. Over time, we started talking about businesses, and then it evolved into a strong bond. I view him as a mentor, teacher, partner, and friend, all in one."* In 2024 he reveals they spent 20 years of summers together at Star Island, Minnesota, including the fishing-guide story that frames his "fish where the fish are" principle. The choice is revealed by time allocation — he spent summers with Munger, not with LPs or conferences.

- **Keeping a low public profile.** Observable behavior: very few interviews, no TV appearances, book published only in Chinese originally, fund closed to new capital for most of its history, refuses to discuss specific holdings except BYD. Stated reason (2013): his goal is *"by the time I finished my career, one of the best track records on a risk-adjusted basis"* — not AUM, not fame.

- **Refusing most outside capital.** PRIMARY, 2013: *"The fund has been closed to new investors for nine years... I usually don't want to increase our size. My ambition has never been to run the largest fund."* This is a business decision that actively *costs* him fee income every year. The revealed value: he believes capacity is finite in concentrated investing and that size destroys returns, and he is willing to pay for that belief in forgone fees. Consistent with the no-management-fee structure.

## Action vs stated belief — tensions (high signal)

1. **"I don't talk about holdings" vs. BYD.** He repeatedly says he doesn't discuss positions, then in the same speeches discusses BYD at length. Interpretation: BYD has become too public to hide, so he uses it strategically as the one approved case study. Not a hypocrisy — a revealed preference for teaching through one worked example rather than opening the book.

2. **"Cheap securities first, then great businesses" — a drift he admits.** 2013: *"I started out looking for cheap securities... over time... I morphed into finding strong businesses at bargain prices. I still have a streak in me that favors finding really cheap securities — I just can't help it!"* This is Li Lu openly describing a philosophy drift from Graham toward Buffett-Munger. It is not a contradiction so much as a maturation, but worth noting because the early track record was built on the *Graham* method and the later track record on the *Munger* method — two different skill sets dressed in one name.

3. **"Macro is what we must accept, micro is where we make a difference" (2024) vs. 40 minutes of macro analysis in the same 2024 speech.** The speech's first three sections are macro-political argument about China's middle-income trap, Hong Kong, US-China relations. The micro-discipline is preached; the public speaking time is macro. Action reveals that thinking about macro is where his attention actually goes in late career, even as he tells his audience to ignore it. This may partly reflect audience (Peking University) but it is a real gap between prescription and demonstrated attention.

4. **"Circle of competence" vs. broad industry picks.** He has invested in Chinese tech (Baidu), US banks (BAC), global search/ads (Alphabet), memory (Micron), e-commerce (PDD), Chinese autos/batteries (BYD), and conglomerates (Berkshire). Either the circle is very large, or the circle is specifically "businesses I've personally studied to ownership-level depth regardless of sector," which is the more honest reading from his own words.

5. **Shorting: "one of my worst mistakes" (2013) vs. "it was a mistake... but... I learn something" (same interview).** He frames it as both a serious error and a tuition payment. Tension is mild but worth flagging: he is willing to absorb category-level mistakes as learning, which is itself a revealed belief about how one should relate to one's errors.
