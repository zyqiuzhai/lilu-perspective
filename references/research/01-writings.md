## Core book: *Modernization, Civilization, Value Investing and China* (文明、现代化、价值投资与中国)

Li Lu explicitly names this book in his 2024 Peking University speech: *"In my book, Civilization, Modernization, Value Investing and China, I divide the evolution of civilization into three stages: 1.0 (hunter-gatherer civilization), 2.0 (agricultural civilization), and 3.0 (modern science-technological civilization). The transitional phase between stages 2.0 and 3.0, which I call stage 2.5, is where China currently stands."* (PRIMARY, high confidence)

The book's backbone is a long essay Li Lu has been writing/revising since at least 2014, originally titled **"A Discussion on Modernization" (论现代化)**. In the 2015 Peking University lecture he explicitly references it and gives the URL: *"I compiled my thoughts, observations and insights in writing a long thesis entitled A Discussion on Modernization By Li Lu. If this question interests you, you may want to read it. You can find it online at http://www.himcap.com/articles/a_discussion_on_modernization.html"* (PRIMARY, 2015 lecture).

### Core arguments of the book (reconstructed from the 2015 and 2024 lectures, which summarize it)

1. **Three-stage civilization framework** — Civilization 1.0 (hunter-gatherer, ~15,000 years ago), Civilization 2.0 (agriculture + animal husbandry, ~9,000 BCE onward), Civilization 3.0 (modern science & technology + free market economy, ~1750s onward). China is currently at **Civilization 2.5** — an interim state between 2.0 and 3.0.

2. **The hockey-stick chart** — Li Lu uses Ian Morris's *Social Development* (2010) data on 16,000 years of human energy capture to show that for ~99% of human history, GDP per capita was effectively flat; only in the last ~300 years does the curve turn vertical. "This can be explained mathematically by the magical powers of compounding, and indicates that the economic pattern of sustained, long-term compounding growth is a modern phenomenon, which had never previously occurred in the recorded human history of the past 16,000 years." (2015 lecture)

3. **Two formulas for wealth creation**: **1+1>2** (Smith/Ricardo free-trade synergy from division of labor and goods exchange) and **1+1>4** (Li Lu's own framing: knowledge exchange has no trade-off — both parties keep their ideas while gaining the other's — so the combinatorial effect is multiplicative, not additive). Modern science & technology plus free market = 1+1>4 accelerant that is the engine of Civilization 3.0.

4. **The Iron Law of Civilization 3.0 / Iron Law of Modernization**: *"when there are two competing market systems, with the interaction of two forces of 1+1>2 and 1+1>4; the one with greater trade volume will see greater growth. When one system has greater volume, it will have greater velocity in its growth, and eventually create a single system."* The biggest market eventually becomes the only market. This is Li Lu's own coinage and he repeats it emphatically (red-text in the 2015 lecture PDF).

5. **Essence of modernization**: "Through market mechanisms, modern science & technology continue to produce an increasing number and variety of products at falling costs. This helps meet insatiable human demand and promote sustained cumulative economic growth. This is the essence (nature) of modernization." (2015 lecture)

6. **China's path**: China's failures from 1840–1978 are explained as 150 years of wandering off the "main path" of Civilization 3.0 (Self-Strengthening Movement, Republican era, Maoist planned economy). Only after 1978 did China "return to the core of Civilization 3.0: free market + modern science & technology." Political system and culture are *not* the essence of 3.0; free market + S&T is. Li Lu argues the probability China reverses course is very low because the cost of deviation is immense, and a mature civilization won't accept indefinite decline.

## Core repeated claims (≥3 occurrences = real beliefs)

Each of these is stated across all three primary English sources (2013, 2015, 2024) and is therefore high-confidence Li Lu core belief (PRIMARY).

1. **Stocks are the only asset class with reliable long-term compound growth after inflation.** — Uses Jeremy Siegel's 1802–2014 US data (stocks 6.7% real, $1 → $1.03M; cash $1 → $0.05) in both the 2015 lecture and echoes the logic in 2024. Appears in 2013 G&D more briefly. (3+ occurrences)

2. **Value investing has exactly four (later: six) principles; the first three are Graham's, the fourth is Buffett's circle of competence.** — Graham's trio: (a) stock is fractional ownership, (b) Mr. Market serves you, it doesn't guide you, (c) margin of safety. Buffett's addition: circle of competence. In 2024 he adds two more: (5) **"Fish where the fish are"** (attributed to Munger via a fishing-guide story), and (6) his own contribution: **wealth = your share of global purchasing power; invest in the most dynamic companies in the most vibrant economies**. Stated in 2015 lecture, restated in 2024, core framing in 2013. (3+ occurrences)

3. **The "right way / main path" (正道) vs. shortcuts.** — Central metaphor of the 2015 lecture, repeated in 2024. "On this wide open main path of investing you find very little traffic... Investors take shortcuts because the right and main path takes too long." Value investing = 正道, momentum/speculation = 歧路. (2+ in writings; pervasive across his talks)

4. **Two ethical obligations for money managers**: (a) **intellectual honesty — seek truth and wisdom, refuse to let "where you sit determine how you think"** (屁股决定脑袋); (b) **fiduciary duty is innate, "part of someone's DNA"** — treat every penny like your parents' lifetime savings. Stated with unusual force in 2015 ("I tend to think that fiduciary duty is something innate, part of someone's DNA. I know some who have it and some don't. Those born without cannot acquire it later by any means or through any effort."), reaffirmed in 2024. (2+ occurrences, very high conviction)

5. **Intellectual honesty = four-part epistemic discipline**: know what you know; know what you don't know; know what you don't need to know; know that there are unknown unknowns. Appears verbatim in 2013 G&D: *"The most important thing in our business is intellectual honesty. What I mean is four different things..."* Echoed throughout 2015 and 2024 lectures via "circle of competence" framing. (3+ occurrences)

6. **"Cheap" is how you start; "great business cheap" is where you evolve to.** — 2013: "I started out looking for cheap securities... over time, I morphed into finding strong businesses at bargain prices." 2024: "I bought my first stock in 1993, starting with cheap companies... slowly transitioning from seeking cheap companies to seeking quality yet cheap companies." Exact same autobiographical arc recounted twice, 11 years apart. (2+ occurrences, PRIMARY)

7. **The macro is what you accept; the micro is where you can make a difference.** — Core thesis of the 2024 lecture, quoted verbatim multiple times in that speech. Li Lu attributes this lesson to Munger's life. (Strongest statement in 2024.)

8. **Three conditions for selling**: (a) you made a mistake — sell immediately; (b) valuation swings to extreme bubble territory; (c) you find a better opportunity (opportunity cost). Stated identically in 2013 G&D and in the 2024 Q&A. (2 occurrences, exact parallel)

9. **Modern capital markets are a credibility system, not a capital system.** — Elaborated in depth in 2024 via the Venice → Netherlands → Britain historical chain. Banks provide capital; capital markets provide *credibility* via intermediaries (lawyers, analysts, auditors, public-market investors), which is what converts small savings into productive investment. This is Li Lu's strongest macro-institutional claim.

10. **Hong Kong is Britain's gift to China, analogous to the Dutch financial system transplanted to Britain after 1688.** — Novel historical framing in the 2024 lecture. Flagged as a distinctive self-coined metaphor.

11. **Wealth is the proportion of your purchasing power in the economy you consume in** — not an absolute number. British noble castles and "ten-thousand yuan households" examples. Li Lu explicitly labels this the **sixth principle of value investing** and his own personal contribution to the Graham-Buffett-Munger lineage (2024).

## Self-coined terms / distinctive framings

- **Civilization 1.0 / 2.0 / 2.5 / 3.0** — his own taxonomy. The "2.5" label for the transitional stage (China today, also where Brazil/Argentina got stuck) is distinctively his.
- **Iron Law of Civilization 3.0 / Iron Law of Modernization** — his own phrasing for the "largest market becomes the only market" principle.
- **1+1>2 vs 1+1>4** — his own shorthand for goods-exchange vs. knowledge-exchange synergies. Repeated across multiple talks.
- **正道 / "the right way and main path"** — borrowed Confucian framing, but he applies it distinctively to investing.
- **"Where you sit determines how you think" (屁股决定脑袋)** — Chinese idiom, but Li Lu elevates it to an epistemic hazard specific to the asset management industry.
- **"Compound growth" as a civilizational, not just financial, phenomenon** — his frequent move from compound interest (Einstein's "eighth wonder") to compound civilizational growth as the defining feature of modernity.
- **"Lying flat" bureaucrats / "involution"** — in 2024, he uses these colloquial Chinese terms to describe stagnation.
- **"Fiduciary DNA"** — biological framing of fiduciary duty as innate and unlearnable.
- **"Every node on the chain is both chicken and egg"** — 2024 framing for economic chain reactions.
- **"Wealth = share of purchasing power"** — his self-described sixth value-investing principle.

## Reading list / intellectual lineage

Directly named and repeatedly cited by Li Lu (PRIMARY):

- **Jeremy Siegel**, *Stocks for the Long Run* / *Future for Investors* — his go-to dataset for the 200-year stock return argument. Cited in 2015 lecture explicitly.
- **Ian Morris**, *Social Development* (2010) and *Why the West Rules — For Now* — the source of the 16,000-year "hockey stick" chart and the Eastern/Western civilization comparison. Cited by name in 2015 lecture.
- **Adam Smith**, *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) — cited explicitly in 2024 for the market-economy consensus.
- **David Ricardo** — free-trade comparative advantage, cited in 2015 lecture.
- **Benjamin Graham**, *The Intelligent Investor* (1949) — explicitly named as the source of the first three value-investing principles.
- **Warren Buffett's letters and writings** — Li Lu in 2013 says "I learn more about him from his writings and deeds than through interpersonal interactions."
- **Charlie Munger** — personal mentor; Li Lu wrote the preface to the Chinese edition of *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (widely known fact; Li Lu organized and championed the Chinese edition — flagged as context, needs primary-source confirmation).
- **John Maynard Keynes** — cited in 2024 as an under-recognized value investor who managed King's College Cambridge endowment 1921–1946. Keynes is held up alongside Graham as a founding figure.
- **John Templeton** — cited in 2024 as the key figure who spread value investing globally; the "buy every stock under $1 in 1939" story is recounted.
- **Lee Kuan Yew** — cited in 2024 for governance methodology: "resolutely replicate proven practices and avoid those that have proven unfeasible."
- **Deng Xiaoping** — "practice is the criterion for testing truth" and "crossing the river by feeling the stones" are invoked as his governance epistemology.

Notably absent from his explicit citation list: academic finance (Fama, efficient markets), behavioral finance (Kahneman/Thaler), modern portfolio theory. He treats all of these as either wrong or irrelevant.

## Evidence from local primary sources (direct quotes)

### *The Prospect of Value Investing in China* (Peking University, Oct 23 2015)

On the main path vs shortcuts: *"On this wide open main path of investing you find very little traffic and wonder where everybody is. You need only look at the 'heterodox paths' of shortcuts with traffic backed up for miles. Investors take shortcuts because the right and main path takes too long."*

On the Iron Law (emphasized in red in the original PDF): *"the iron law of modernization. It states that when there are two competing market systems, with the interaction of two forces of 1+1>2 and 1+1>4; the one with greater trade volume will see greater growth. When one system has greater volume, it will have greater velocity in its growth, and eventually create a single system."*

On fiduciary DNA: *"I tend to think that fiduciary duty is something innate, part of someone's DNA. I know some who have it and some don't. Those born without cannot acquire it later by any means or through any effort."*

On China-specific optimism: *"I believe China is at interim stage between Civilization 2.0 and Civilization 3.0. Let's call it Civilization 2.5."*

### *Global Value Investing in Our Era* (Peking University, Dec 7 2024)

On wealth: *"the essence of wealth is the proportion of purchasing power in the economy, and the goal of value investing is to hold shares of the most dynamic companies in the most vibrant economies, thereby preserving and growing wealth. This principle is also the experience and contribution based on our practice at Himalaya Capital over the past thirty years."*

On Hong Kong as Britain's gift: *"Britain left China a gift — Hong Kong... If the relationship between the Netherlands and Britain was a merger of equals, then the relationship between China and Hong Kong is more like an acquisition, and the benefits of acquisitions are often not cherished enough."*

On credibility systems: *"The capital market provides not just capital but a system based on credibility. Banks can provide capital but cannot create that type of system."*

On macro vs micro: *"the macro environment is what we must accept, and the micro environment is where we can and should make a significant difference. Engaging in value investing allows us to breathe in sync with the times and grow alongside it."*

On Chinese governance critique (notably direct for Li Lu): *"When phenomena such as 'arbitrary law enforcement,' 'selective law enforcement,' and 'cross-jurisdictional arrests' occur, do entrepreneurs have the right to defend their legitimate interests through the legal system?"*

### *Graham & Doddsville Interview* (Spring 2013)

On intellectual honesty (verbatim): *"The most important thing in our business is intellectual honesty. What I mean is four different things: know what you know, know what you don't know, know what you don't have to know, and realize that there is always a possibility that 'you don't know that you don't know.'"*

On the "too hard" pile (Munger's framing, adopted by Li Lu): *"There are basically three buckets that Charlie has. 'Yes', 'no', or 'too hard'. Most of the things fall in 'too hard.'"*

On his evolution: *"I started out looking for cheap securities... But over time, if you are interested in businesses in addition to securities, you begin to become a student of businesses... I morphed into finding strong businesses at bargain prices. I still have a streak in me that favors finding really cheap securities — I just can't help it!"*

On crisis behavior: *"That's why people freeze in the midst of a crisis. People freeze because they were not intellectually honest before. They never quite distinguished certain issues or questions and put them into the appropriate basket."*

On shorting (why he quit in ~2004): *"Three things about shorting make it a miserable business. On the long side, you have 100% downside but unlimited upside. On the short side, you have 100% upside and unlimited downside... the best short has some element of fraud. However, a fraud can be perpetrated for a long time... Lastly, it screws up your mind."*

On compensation structure (revealing of values): *"We don't take any management fee. We provide a 6% return for free to our investors and then take 25% after that. I don't invest anything outside of the fund. I put all of my investment capital into my funds."* (Original Buffett Partnership formula.)

## Contradictions / tensions found

1. **Fiduciary duty is "innate DNA" vs. value investing is teachable.** He simultaneously claims the key ethical trait cannot be acquired ("those born without cannot acquire it later by any means or through any effort") and runs a decade-long educational course at Peking University to produce ethical value investors. Unresolved.

2. **"The macro is what you must accept" vs. spending ~75% of the 2024 speech on macro analysis.** He explicitly tells students not to research macro, then delivers a ~15,000-word macro analysis of China's middle-income trap. His resolution is that macro understanding is defensive — you need it to hold through drawdowns — but the emphasis is noticeably in tension with his stated method.

3. **Strong pro-China structural optimism (2015) vs. pointed critique of Chinese governance (2024).** In 2015 he is confident China will stay on the Civilization 3.0 track. In 2024 he itemizes the property-rights problems, arbitrary law enforcement, Hong Kong backsliding, state-bank dominance, and 40% consumption share. This is not necessarily a contradiction — he reconciles it by saying deviation is a "brief" episode in historical time but painful in human time — but the shift in tone is stark and is itself signal.

4. **Circle of competence is small and defined by interest** (2013) vs. **"fish in small lakes where there is no competition"** (2024) vs. **"find the most dynamic companies in the most vibrant economies"** (2024). These are roughly compatible but point in slightly different directions: the fishing metaphor favors obscure undervalued niches; the "vibrant economies" sixth principle favors scale and creativity. Li Lu himself seems to have migrated from the first toward the second.

5. **He preaches margin of safety and "avoid wrong decisions" but held BYD through a >80% drawdown**, by his own admission. This is consistent with circle-of-competence doctrine (if you know the value, volatility is not risk) but is inconsistent with classic Graham margin-of-safety discipline. He treats this as the deepest test of whether your circle of competence is real.

**Methodological note**: Li Lu is an unusually disciplined writer/speaker — he repeats core claims verbatim across decades, which makes the "≥3 occurrences = real belief" threshold easy to meet for the central framework but may overweight the framework relative to newer, less-repeated ideas (e.g., the sixth principle about purchasing power is only stated clearly in 2024).
